# Second Conduct Rule (s.21, Cap 619)

## Overview

The Second Conduct Rule prohibits undertakings with **substantial market power** from abusing that power by engaging in conduct that has the object or effect of preventing, restricting, or distorting competition in Hong Kong.

## Key Provisions

### s.21 — Prohibition
An undertaking that has **substantial market power** in a market must not abuse that power by engaging in conduct that has the object or effect of harming competition in Hong Kong.

### Substantial Market Power (SMP)

Market power is the ability to profitably sustain prices above competitive levels or restrict output below competitive levels for a significant period.

**Relevant factors** (Competition Commission Guidelines):
- Market share (presumption of SMP: 50%+, per Tribunal guidance)
- Barriers to entry and expansion
- Buyer power (countervailing power of customers)
- Vertical integration
- Access to essential facilities
- Market structure dynamics

### Abusive Conduct — Exclusionary

Common categories of abuse:

1. **Predatory Pricing** — Pricing below cost to eliminate competitors
2. **Margin Squeeze** — Vertical integrated firm setting wholesale price high and retail price low to squeeze efficient competitors
3. **Refusal to Supply** — Refusing to supply an essential input to a competitor
4. **Tying and Bundling** — Forcing customers to buy additional products
5. **Exclusive Dealing** — Requiring customers to deal exclusively
6. **Loyalty Rebates** — Conditional rebates that foreclose competitors

### Abusive Conduct — Exploitative
- Unfair purchase/selling prices
- Unfair trading conditions

## Market Definition (preliminary step)

Before assessing SMP, define the relevant market:
- **Product market**: Substitutability on demand and supply side
- **Geographic market**: Hong Kong (domestic market focus)

## Typical Analysis Framework

1. Define the relevant market
2. Does the undertaking have substantial market power?
3. Identify the conduct in question
4. Does the conduct have anti-competitive object or effect?
5. Consider objective justification/efficiency defences

## Defences

- **Objective justification**: Conduct is proportionate to a legitimate business goal
- **Efficiency defence**: Conduct produces efficiencies that outweigh anti-competitive effects
- **Submitting to regulatory requirements**: Conduct required by law

## Enforcement

- **Competition Commission**: Investigation, enforcement, commitment agreements
- **Competition Tribunal**: Pecuniary penalties, directions, injunctions
- **Maximum penalty**: Up to 10% of Hong Kong gross turnover for each year of infringement (up to 3 years)
