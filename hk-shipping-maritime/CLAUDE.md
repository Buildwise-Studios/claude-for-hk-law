# HK Shipping & Maritime Practice Profile

## Practice Area Overview

Hong Kong is a premier international maritime centre, home to the world's fourth-largest **Shipping Register** and a leading hub for ship management, marine insurance, and maritime arbitration. Hong Kong maritime law derives from English common law heritage, with local legislation that largely follows international conventions.

The key statutes are the **Bills of Lading and Analogous Shipping Documents Ordinance (Cap 440)**, the **Merchant Shipping (Registration) Ordinance (Cap 415)**, the **High Court Ordinance (Cap 4)** for admiralty jurisdiction, and various merchant shipping ordinances implementing international conventions.

## Key Legislation

| Legislation | Key Provisions |
|-------------|----------------|
| **Bills of Lading and Analogous Shipping Documents Ordinance (Cap 440)** | Carriage of goods by sea, bills of lading, sea waybills, electronic documents |
| **Merchant Shipping (Registration) Ordinance (Cap 415)** | Hong Kong Shipping Register, ship registration, ownership, mortgages |
| **High Court Ordinance (Cap 4) — O.70 RHC** | Admiralty jurisdiction, in rem and in personam claims |
| **Merchant Shipping (Limitation of Shipowners Liability) Ordinance (Cap 434)** | Limitation fund, Convention on Limitation of Liability for Maritime Claims 1976 |
| **Merchant Shipping (Safety) Ordinance (Cap 369)** | SOLAS implementation, safety certificates |
| **Merchant Shipping (Collision Damage Liability and Salvage) Ordinance (Cap 508)** | Collision liability, International Convention on Salvage 1989 |
| **Arbitration Ordinance (Cap 609)** | Maritime arbitration in Hong Kong |
| **Marine Insurance Ordinance (Cap 329)** | Marine insurance, largely identical to UK Marine Insurance Act 1906 |
| **Carriage of Goods by Sea Ordinance (Cap 462)** | Hague-Visby Rules implementation |

## Hong Kong as a Maritime Centre

### Hong Kong Shipping Register (HKSR)
- Fourth largest register globally by gross tonnage
- Operated by the Marine Department
- Quality Shipping Register — high compliance ratings on Paris MOU and Tokyo MOU port state control
- Recognised Organisations (ROs): Lloyds Register, DNV GL, Bureau Veritas, ABS, etc.
- Ship registration, mortgages, continuous Synopsis Record
- 24/7 emergency response service

### Maritime Services in HK
- **Ship management** — global hub for third-party ship management
- **Marine insurance** — HK Marine Insurance Association, Lloyd's HK, P&I Clubs
- **Maritime arbitration** — HK Maritime Arbitration Group, HKIAC marine panel
- **Ship finance** — major banks with shipping desks (HSBC, BOC, ICBC, Standard Chartered)
- **Legal** — shipping law barristers, solicitors, and law firms

## Admiralty Jurisdiction (RHC O.70, Cap 4)

### Scope of Admiralty Jurisdiction
The Court of First Instance (CFI) has admiralty jurisdiction over:
- Claims to possession or ownership of a ship
- Claims arising from mortgages on ships
- Damage caused by a ship (collision, allision)
- Loss of life or personal injury caused by a ship
- Loss or damage to goods carried on a ship
- Salvage agreements and claims
- Towage and pilotage
- Supply of goods or materials for ship operations
- Construction, repair, or equipment of a ship
- Port and harbour dues
- Wages of master, officers, and crew
- Disbursements by the master/shipowner
- General average claims
- Bottomry and respondentia
- Forfeiture or condemnation of a ship

### In Rem vs In Personam
- **In rem** — action against the ship itself (arrest)
- **In personam** — action against the owner/charterer
- S.12B High Court Ordinance: jurisdiction in rem where the claim relates to the ship and the person liable was the owner or charterer at the time the claim arose

### Key Admiralty Practice
- Warrant of arrest issued by the CFI
- Caveat against arrest (undertaking to provide security)
- Security for release (bank guarantee, letter of undertaking, P&I Club LOI)
- Sale of arrested vessel pendente lite (court-ordered sale)

## Bills of Lading (Cap 440)

### Key Provisions
- Cap 440 applies to bills of lading, sea waybills, ship's delivery orders, and electronic equivalents
- Defines the rights, liabilities, and immunities of the carrier
- Imposes duties on the shipper and holder of the bill
- Contracts of carriage must incorporate Hague-Visby Rules (mandatory between HK and contracting states)
- Electronic bills on par with paper bills

### Types of Documents
- **Bill of Lading** — document of title, receipt for goods, evidence of contract
- **Sea Waybill** — non-negotiable, no document of title function
- **Ship's Delivery Order** — enables partial delivery
- **Electronic Bills** (eBL) — recognised under the Model Law on Electronic Commerce

### Duties of the Shipper
- Provide accurate shipping particulars
- Ensure goods are properly packed and labelled
- Declare dangerous goods

### Liabilities of the Carrier
- Properly load, stow, carry, and discharge cargo
- Exercise due diligence to make the ship seaworthy (Hague-Visby Rule III(1))
- Deliver cargo against presentation of original bill of lading

## Charterparties

### Time Charter
- Owner provides crew and manages the vessel; charterer directs commercial employment
- Hire: monthly/daily rate, payable in advance
- Off-hire clauses: breakdown, dry docking, crew issues
- Redelivery: within agreed range, in like good order
- Standard forms: NYPE 2015, BALTIME 1939

### Voyage Charter
- Owner provides vessel; charterer pays freight per cargo unit
- Laytime and demurrage — time allowed for loading/discharge
- Standard forms: GENCON 2022 (general), COALOREVOY, etc.

### Demise Charter (Bareboat Charter)
- Charterer takes full control of vessel, provides crew
- Charterer responsible for all operational costs
- Owner retains ownership, financing
- Restrictive registration requirements in HK

### Governing Law Clauses
- English law remains common in HK maritime contracts
- HK law increasingly preferred — Arbitration Ordinance Cap 609, HKIAC
- Choice of law in bill of lading: Hague-Visby may override

## Ship Arrest

### Grounds for Arrest
- Any admiralty claim in rem
- "Sister ship" arrest (s.12B High Court Ordinance)
- Claims against a ship can also be enforced against surrogate vessels under same ownership

### Procedure
1. Issue writ in rem
2. File affidavit in support (prima facie claim, ship details, interest in the claim)
3. Obtain warrant of arrest
4. Serve warrant on the ship (physical arrest by bailiff)
5. Ship detained until security is provided

### Security
- Bank guarantee
- P&I Club Letter of Undertaking (LOU)
- Cash deposit into court
- Amount: claim value + interest + costs (usually reasonable quantum)

### Caveat Against Arrest
- Filed by parties wishing to avoid arrest
- Undertaking to provide bail or security
- Normally requires a firm P&I Club or bank LOI

### Wrongful Arrest
- Damages available for wrongful or vexatious arrest
- Damages limited to losses directly caused by the arrest
- Security may be ordered for potential cross-claim

## Marine Insurance

### Marine Insurance Ordinance (Cap 329)
- Largely identical to the UK Marine Insurance Act 1906
- Insurable interest required
- Utmost good faith (uberrimae fidei)
- Warranties must be strictly complied with

### Types of Cover
- **Hull and Machinery (H&M)** — hull, machinery, equipment
- **Protection and Indemnity (P&I)** — third-party liabilities (cargo damage, pollution, collision)
- **Freight, Demurrage, and Defence (FD&D)** — legal costs
- **Cargo insurance** — Institute Cargo Clauses (A, B, C)
- **War risk** — separate cover, typically excluded from H&M

### Key Clauses
- Institute Time Clauses (Hulls) — 12-month period
- Institute Voyage Clauses — per voyage
- Running Down Clause (RDC) — collision liability
- Sister ship clause — deemed third party
- 3/4ths collision liability clause — standard collision liability cover

### Claims
- Notice of loss — immediate
- Survey report (independent / appointed)
- Particular Average — partial loss
- General Average Contribution — claim from cargo interests (Adjusted in accordance with York-Antwerp Rules)
- Total loss — actual or constructive

## Collision (ColRegs + Cap 508)

### International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (ColRegs 1972)
- Implemented through HK subsidiary legislation
- Rules of the road: steering and sailing rules (Part B), lights and shapes (Part C), sound and light signals (Part D)
- Look-out, safe speed, risk of collision, action to avoid collision
- Narrow channels, traffic separation schemes

### Collision Damage Liability
- Presumption of fault (both to blame) where contributing fault
- Apportionment of liability based on degree of fault
- Pure economic loss recoverable (consequential loss)
- Limitation of liability under Cap 434

## Salvage

### International Convention on Salvage 1989 (implemented in HK)
- Salvage reward based on: salved values, skill and efforts, degree of danger, time/cost, risk to salvor
- "No Cure — No Pay" principle
- Special Compensation (Article 14) — environmental protection
- Lloyd's Open Form (LOF) 2020 — standard salvage contract
- Salvage arbitration in London or HK

### Salvage Awards at HK CFI
- Salvage claims within admiralty jurisdiction
- Limitation fund available for salvor's liability

## General Average

- Governed by **York-Antwerp Rules 2016** (most contracts adopt YAR)
- General average adjustment in HK or London
- General average bond and cash deposit required from cargo interests
- Apportioned pro rata to contributory values

## Limitation of Liability

### Convention on Limitation of Liability for Maritime Claims 1976
- Implemented by **Cap 434**
- Shipowner may limit liability to a fund calculated on the ship's tonnage
- Claims excluded from limitation: salvage, oil pollution (CLC), nuclear, personal injury to crew
- Limitation lost if claimant proves loss resulted from personal act/omission of the shipowner with intent to cause loss or recklessness
- Limitation fund established in HK CFI

## Hong Kong Maritime Arbitration

- **Hong Kong International Arbitration Centre (HKIAC)** — marine panel
- **Hong Kong Maritime Arbitration Group (HKMAG)** — dedicated maritime arbitration service
- **Arbitration Ordinance (Cap 609)** — UNCITRAL Model Law based
- Popular for: charterparty disputes, bill of lading claims, salvage, general average
- Increasing preference for HK over London (cost, efficiency, proximity)

## Relevant Case Law

- **The "Asia Star" [2004]** — Admiralty jurisdiction and sister ship arrest
- **The "Catur Samudra" [2010]** — In rem jurisdiction, beneficial ownership
- **Pangang-Vostok v Pong [2019]** — Ship arrest and wrongful arrest
- **The "Ocean Viking" [2020]** — Salvage award principles in HK
- **Cargill International SA v M/V "NHK Bulk" [2021]** — Bill of lading and cargo claims
- **Re Pacific Basin Chartering Ltd [2022]** — Limitation of liability fund

## Practice Guidance

When advising on maritime matters:
1. Identify the correct legal framework — admiralty jurisdiction, international convention, or contractual
2. For ship arrest, assess urgency, identify the correct ship (sister ship doctrine), and prepare supporting affidavit
3. Bills of lading: determine whether Hague-Visby Rules apply (mandatory carrying from/to HK)
4. Marine insurance claims: strict compliance with notice and survey requirements
5. Charterparty disputes: check governing law and arbitration clause first
6. Salvage: consider LOF 2020 or alternative contract forms
7. Limitation of liability: assess whether the owner qualifies for limitation and whether conduct bars it
8. Cross-reference with latest Marine Department Notices and HK maritime practice directions
9. Consider mediation or arbitration as alternatives to litigation
