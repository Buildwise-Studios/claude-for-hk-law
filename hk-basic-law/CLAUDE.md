# HK Basic Law Practice Profile

## Practice Area Overview

The Hong Kong Basic Law is the constitutional document of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region (HKSAR), enacted by the National People's Congress (NPC) on 4 April 1990 and effective from 1 July 1997. It enshrines the "one country, two systems" principle and governs all aspects of Hong Kong's legal, political, and economic systems.

## Structure

The Basic Law consists of:
- **9 Chapters** and 160 Articles
- **3 Annexes** (Annex I: Method for Selection of the Chief Executive; Annex II: Method for Formation of the Legislative Council and Voting Procedures; Annex III: National Laws to be applied in the HKSAR)
- **Preamble** establishing the "one country, two systems" principle

### Chapter Outline
1. General Principles (Arts 1–11)
2. Relationship between the Central Authorities and the HKSAR (Arts 12–23)
3. Fundamental Rights and Duties of Residents (Arts 24–42)
4. Political Structure (Arts 43–104)
5. Economy (Arts 105–136)
6. Education, Science, Culture, Sports, Religion, Labour and Social Services (Arts 136–149)
7. External Affairs (Arts 150–157)
8. Interpretation and Amendment of the Basic Law (Arts 158–159)
9. Supplementary Provisions (Art 160)

## Key Articles

### Article 8 — Preservation of Common Law
"The laws previously in force in Hong Kong, that is, the common law, rules of equity, ordinances, subordinate legislation and customary law shall be maintained, except for any that contravene this Law, and subject to any amendment by the legislature of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region."

This is the foundational provision preserving Hong Kong's common law system.

### Article 18 — Laws in Force
Specifies which laws apply in the HKSAR:
1. The Basic Law
2. Laws previously in force (as preserved by Art 8)
3. Laws enacted by the HKSAR legislature
4. National laws listed in Annex III (applied by notification or promulgation locally)

**Critical**: The Standing Committee of the NPC (NPCSC) may add to or remove from Annex III after consultation with the Basic Law Committee and the HKSAR Government. National laws relating to defence, foreign affairs, and matters outside the limits of HKSAR autonomy shall be applied.

### Article 84 — Judicial Reference
Courts may refer to precedents of other common law jurisdictions. CFA and High Court judgments are binding on lower courts. Courts shall adjudicate in accordance with the Basic Law and laws of the HKSAR.

### Article 158 — Power of Interpretation
- The power of interpretation of the Basic Law is vested in the NPCSC
- HKSAR courts may interpret provisions within the limits of HKSAR autonomy
- For provisions concerning affairs within the responsibility of the Central Government (defence, foreign affairs), the CFA must seek NPCSC interpretation
- NPCSC interpretations are binding on HKSAR courts

### Article 159 — Amendment
- Power of amendment is vested in the NPC
- Amendment bills require the consent of the NPCCC and two-thirds of HK deputies to the NPC, two-thirds of the LegCo, and the CE
- No amendment may contravene the established basic policies

## NPCSC Interpretations

The NPCSC has issued five interpretations of the Basic Law (as of 2025):

1. **1999 — Right of Abode (Ng Ka Ling)** — Interpretation on Arts 22 and 24(3), overturning the CFA's approach to residency rights
2. **2004 — Constitutional Development** — Interpretation on Annexes I and II, establishing the procedure for electoral reform
3. **2005 — Term of Office of the CE** — Interpretation on Art 53, confirming a successor CE serves the remainder of the term
4. **2011 — Sovereign Immunity** — Interpretation on Art 13 and 19, confirming the Central Government's authority over state immunity
5. **2016 — Oath-taking** — Interpretation on Art 104, clarifying the oath-taking requirements for LegCo members and disqualification consequences

**Impact on Practice**: NPCSC interpretations are binding on HKSAR courts and have retrospective effect (from the date the Basic Law came into effect). They must be incorporated into legal arguments where the interpreted provisions are engaged.

## Annex III National Laws

Current Annex III national laws applicable in the HKSAR (non-exhaustive):

- National Security Law (NSL) — promulgated 30 June 2020
- Law on the National Flag and National Emblem
- Law on the National Anthem
- Law on the Territorial Sea and the Contiguous Zone
- Law on the Exclusive Economic Zone and the Continental Shelf
- Law on the Garrisoning of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region
- Law on Judicial Immunity from Execution Concerning the Property of Foreign Central Banks
- Regulations on the Use of Insignia of the United Nations and other International Organizations
- Law on the National Defence and Armed Forces
- Several others relating to foreign affairs, diplomatic privileges, and international organizations

## Relationship between Basic Law, Ordinances, and Common Law

```
Basic Law (supreme constitutional document)
    ↓
National Laws in Annex III (applied in HK)
    ↓
Ordinances enacted by LegCo (must not contravene BL)
    ↓
Subsidiary Legislation (regulations, rules, orders)
    ↓
Common Law & Equity (preserved by Art 8)
```

- **Supremacy**: Any HK ordinance that contravenes the Basic Law is invalid
- **Review**: HK courts have the power to review legislation for compliance with the BL (though final interpretation rests with NPCSC for central government matters)
- **Common Law preservation**: The common law remains in force except where inconsistent with the BL

## National Security Law Implications

The NSL (promulgated under Annex III, 30 June 2020) established:
- Four offences: secession, subversion, terrorism, collusion with foreign forces
- The Committee for Safeguarding National Security (led by the CE)
- National Security Department of the HK Police
- Designated judges for NSL cases
- Parallel prosecution rights exercisable by the Secretary for Justice
- The power of the CE to appoint/remove designated judges

**Practice Implications**:
- NSL overrides conflicting HK law
- No jury trials for NSL offences (judge-only)
- National security considerations can be raised in other proceedings
- Legal practitioners must take the NSL into account when advising clients on any matter touching national security
- The NSL has extraterritorial effect for certain offences

## ICAC and the Basic Law

The Independent Commission Against Corruption (ICAC) is established under the Basic Law (Art 57) and the ICAC Ordinance (Cap 204). It operates independently of the civil service and reports directly to the CE. The ICAC's powers and the Basic Law's guarantee of fundamental rights must be balanced in practice.

## Basic Law Litigation in the CFA

Landmark CFA cases involving the Basic Law:

- **Ng Ka Ling v Director of Immigration (1999)** — Right of abode, first CFA interpretation request to NPCSC
- **Lau Cheong Wa v HKSAR (2015)** — Constitutional review of Criminal Procedure Ordinance
- **Kwok Cheuk Kin v Secretary for Constitutional and Mainland Affairs (2022)** — LegCo candidate eligibility and the electoral system
- **HKSAR v Lai Chee Ying (2021)** — NSL prosecution, judge-only trial, bail provisions
- **Tam Sze Leung v Secretary for Justice (2022)** — Pre-trial restraint orders and NSL procedural safeguards
- **HKSAR v Ma Chun Wai (2023)** — National security offence sentencing guidelines

## Practice Guidance

When advising on Basic Law matters:
1. Always check the original Chinese text — the Chinese version prevails in case of discrepancy
2. Consider whether an NPCSC interpretation is relevant or likely
3. Analyze whether the issue concerns "autonomy" matters (courts decide) or "central government" matters (CFA must refer)
4. Review applicable Annex III national laws alongside HK ordinances
5. Assess fundamental rights under Chapter III against permissible limitations
6. Consider NSL implications for any matter with a national security dimension
7. Check the latest BL-related CFA and Court of Appeal judgments via HKLII or Westlaw Asia
