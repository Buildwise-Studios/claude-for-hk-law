# Hong Kong Legal System — Overview

## One Country, Two Systems

Since the handover on 1 July 1997, Hong Kong has operated as a Special Administrative Region (HKSAR) of the People's Republic of China under the principle of **"One Country, Two Systems"**. The **Basic Law** (adopted 4 April 1990, effective 1 July 1997) serves as Hong Kong's constitutional document, guaranteeing a high degree of autonomy for 50 years after handover (i.e., until 2047). Key features:

- Independent judiciary
- Common law system separate from mainland China's civil law system
- Separate legal and judicial systems
- Independent courts of final adjudication (Article 85 Basic Law)
- Continued application of pre-1997 laws not inconsistent with the Basic Law

## Sources of Law

Under Articles 8, 18, and 84 of the Basic Law, the sources of Hong Kong law are:

### 1. The Basic Law
The constitutional document of the HKSAR. It prevails over all other laws. Any legislation inconsistent with the Basic Law is invalid.

### 2. Common Law and Rules of Equity
Hong Kong inherited the English common law system before 1997. Since handover, Hong Kong has developed its own independent common law, though English and other Commonwealth decisions remain **persuasive**. Courts are bound by:

- Pre-1997: UK Privy Council decisions were binding as the highest court of appeal
- Post-1997: HK courts develop their own jurisprudence; UK Supreme Court decisions are persuasive only

### 3. Ordinances (Primary Legislation)
Statutes enacted by the Legislative Council (LegCo). Each Ordinance is assigned a Chapter number (Cap). Examples:

- Companies Ordinance (Cap 622)
- Contracts for the Overseas Transfer of Title to Land Ordinance (Cap 214)
- Domestic Violence Ordinance (Cap 189)

Bills are published as three readings in LegCo, passed by a majority vote, and then gazetted as law upon the Chief Executive's assent.

### 4. Subsidiary Legislation
Regulations, rules, bylaws, and orders made under powers delegated by an Ordinance. Also known as delegated legislation. They carry the force of law but can be amended or repealed by the parent Ordinance.

### 5. Customary Law
Limited to certain areas, particularly relating to **New Territories land** (e.g., traditional Chinese customary law governing succession of land in the New Territories prior to 1970). The New Territories Ordinance (Cap 97) preserved certain Chinese customs and usages.

### 6. International Treaties
Over 260 international treaties apply to Hong Kong. They do **not** form part of domestic law unless incorporated by legislation (dualist system). However, courts may have regard to treaty obligations when interpreting legislation.

### 7. National Laws
Certain PRC national laws listed in **Annex III** of the Basic Law apply to Hong Kong. These include laws relating to:

- National flag, national emblem, and national anthem
- Territorial sea and contiguous zone
- Nationality law
- Diplomatic privileges
- **Hong Kong National Security Law (NSL)** — imposed via Annex III in 2020

## Court Structure

### Court of Final Appeal (CFA)
The highest appellate court in Hong Kong. Hears appeals from the Court of Appeal and the Court of First Instance.

- **Composition**: Chief Justice + 3 permanent judges + 1 non-permanent judge (from a panel of non-permanent judges, including overseas common law judges)
- **Leave required**: Application for leave to appeal must be granted by the CFA or the Court of Appeal
- **Test for leave**: A question of great general or public importance, or "substantial and grave injustice"
- **No further appeal**: The CFA is the court of final adjudication (Article 82 Basic Law)

### High Court

#### Court of Appeal (CA)
Hears appeals from the Court of First Instance and the District Court.

- **Composition**: Usually 3 judges; sometimes 2 for interlocutory appeals
- **Civil appeals**: From CFI and DC
- **Criminal appeals**: From CFI (jury trials) and DC

#### Court of First Instance (CFI)
Superior court with unlimited civil and criminal jurisdiction.

- **Civil**: Unlimited monetary jurisdiction; typically handles claims over HK$3M
- **Criminal**: Most serious offences (murder, manslaughter, drug trafficking, etc.) tried by judge and jury
- **Constitutional and administrative law**: Judicial review of government decisions
- **Appeals from tribunals**: From Lands Tribunal, Labour Tribunal (on law), etc.

### District Court (DC)
Intermediate court with limited jurisdiction.

- **Civil**: Up to HK$3,000,000 (or unlimited in equity jurisdiction)
- **Criminal**: Most indictable offences except murder, manslaughter, rape, and a few others
- **No jury**: Judge sits alone
- **Appeals**: To the Court of Appeal

### Magistrates' Courts
Lower criminal courts handling the vast majority of criminal cases.

- **Maximum sentence**: Generally 2 years' imprisonment or HK$100,000 fine (some offences up to 7 years)
- **Summary offences and indictable offences triable summarily**
- **Youth Court**: For offenders aged 10–16
- **Family Court**: Family proceedings under the Matrimonial Causes Ordinance, etc.
- **Appeals**: To the Court of First Instance

### Tribunals
- **Lands Tribunal**: Disputes over land valuation, tenancies, compulsory acquisition
- **Labour Tribunal**: Employment claims (HK$8,000+); no legal representation permitted
- **Small Claims Tribunal**: Claims up to HK$75,000; no legal representation
- **Equal Opportunities Commission** (EOC): Discrimination complaints
- **Obscene Articles Tribunal**: Classification of articles

### Other Courts
- **Coroner's Court**: Inquests into certain deaths
- **Competition Tribunal**: Competition Ordinance (Cap 619) cases

## Judicial Precedent (Stare Decisis)

| Court | Bound by | Precedent value for lower courts |
|---|---|---|
| CFA | Not bound by its own decisions (but follows unless "clearly wrong") | Binding on all lower HK courts |
| CA | CFA | Binding on CFI, DC, Magistrates |
| CFI | CFA, CA | Persuasive for DC, not binding on other CFI judges |
| DC | CFA, CA, CFI | Persuasive for Magistrates |
| Magistrates | All higher courts | Binding only on the same magistrate |

**Key points**:
- **Privy Council decisions pre-1997**: Binding before handover; now **persuasive** only
- **UK Supreme Court post-1997**: **Persuasive** only
- **Other Commonwealth courts** (Australia, Canada, Singapore, etc.): **Persuasive** only
- Mainland Chinese court decisions: **No binding or persuasive value** (different legal system)
- **Commentary**: English textbooks (Halsbury's Laws, Chitty on Contracts, etc.) remain influential but HK textbooks increasingly preferred

## Legislation Process

1. **Policy proposal** by the Executive Council (ExCo) or a LegCo member
2. **Drafting** by the Department of Justice (Law Drafting Division)
3. **First reading** in LegCo — formal introduction, no debate
4. **Second reading** — debate on principle; may be referred to a Bills Committee
5. **Committee stage** — detailed clause-by-clause examination (in Bills Committee)
6. **Third reading** — final vote; simple majority required (except for constitutional amendments to Basic Law)
7. **Gazettal** — Chief Executive signs and gives assent; published in the Gazette
8. **Commencement** — either on gazettal or a date specified in the Ordinance

## Bilingual Legal System

Since 1997, Hong Kong has a **bilingual legal system** (Section 10, Official Languages Ordinance, Cap 5):

- Both **Chinese** and **English** are official languages of the courts
- Legislation is enacted in both languages — both versions are **equally authentic**
- In case of inconsistency, **the Chinese version prevails** (Section 10B(3), Cap 5)
- Court proceedings may be conducted in either language
- Judgments may be delivered in either language

## National Laws Applicable to Hong Kong (Annex III)

The Basic Law provides that PRC national laws listed in Annex III apply to Hong Kong. Current Annex III laws (selected):

- Resolution on the Capital, Calendar, National Anthem, and National Flag
- Law on the National Flag and National Emblem
- Law on the National Anthem
- Law on Territorial Sea and Contiguous Zone
- Law on Nationality
- Regulations on Diplomatic Privileges and Immunities
- Law on the National Flag and National Emblem (revised)
- **Hong Kong National Security Law (NSL)** — enacted 30 June 2020, imposed via Annex III

The NSL created three new offences: secession, subversion, terrorism, and collusion with foreign forces. It established the **Office for Safeguarding National Security** and gives certain powers to the Chief Executive to appoint judges for NSL cases.
