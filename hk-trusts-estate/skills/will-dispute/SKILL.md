# Will Dispute / Family Provision Claim — Skill

## Purpose

Advise on and litigate will disputes and claims under the Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Ordinance (Cap 481), including challenges to will validity, family provision claims, and other estate disputes in Hong Kong.

## Scope

- Grounds for challenging a will
- Lack of testamentary capacity
- Undue influence
- Lack of due execution
- Forgery and fraud
- Family provision claims under Cap 481
- Time limits and procedure
- Mediation of estate disputes
- Costs in probate litigation

## Workflow

### 1. Assessing the Claim

**Types of will dispute**:

| Ground | Basis | Burden of Proof |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| **Lack of testamentary capacity** | Testator did not understand the nature of making a will, the extent of their property, or the claims of potential beneficiaries (Banks v Goodfellow test) | On the challenger (but shifts if suspicious circumstances exist) |
| **Lack of knowledge and approval** | Testator did not know or approve of the will's contents | On the propounder (shifts if reasonable suspicion) |
| **Undue influence** | Coercion or pressure overcame the testator's free will (not mere persuasion) | On the challenger (high threshold) |
| **Fraud / forgery** | Will was forged or testator induced by fraud | On the challenger |
| **Lack of due execution** | Will not properly signed and witnessed (s.5 Cap 30) | On the propounder (prima facie) — shifts if attestation clause present |
| **Revocation** | Will was revoked by marriage, later will, or destruction | On the challenger |
| **Rectification** | Will fails to reflect testator's intentions due to clerical error or misunderstanding of instructions (s.17A Cap 30) | On the applicant |

### 2. Testamentary Capacity (Banks v Goodfellow)

**The test** (affirmed in HK — Bradshaw v Beachly):
1. Understanding the nature of a will and its effects
2. Understanding the extent of the property being disposed of
3. Appreciating the claims of those who might expect to benefit
4. No disorder of mind perverting the sense of right or moral duty

**Evidence of capacity**:
- Medical records and medical opinion (ideally contemporaneous with will execution)
- Solicitor's attendance notes (key evidence — was the golden rule followed?)
- Witness recollections
- Circumstantial evidence (business dealings, correspondence, mental state)
- Surveillance footage, social media posts (contemporaneous evidence of capacity)

**The "Golden Rule"**:
- If there is any doubt about capacity, have a medical practitioner examine the testator and witness execution
- Failure to follow the golden rule does not invalidate the will but is strong evidence against the propounder

### 3. Undue Influence

**Definition**: Coercion that destroys the testator's free will — not mere persuasion, emotional pressure, or influence that does not overbear the will.

**Indicators**:
- Beneficiary involved in the will's preparation
- Testator dependent on the beneficiary for care
- Testator isolated from family and friends
- Sudden and inexplicable change from previous will
- Secrecy surrounding the will
- Relationship of dominance and subservience

**Standard of proof**: The challenger must prove undue influence on the balance of probabilities — but the court requires clear and cogent evidence (high threshold).

### 4. Family Provision Claims under Cap 481

**Eligible applicants** (s.3, Cap 481):

| Category | Status |
|----------|--------|
| **Spouse** | Eligible — includes former spouse not remarried |
| **Child of the deceased** | Eligible — includes adult children |
| **Person treated as child of the family** | Eligible — stepchildren, adopted children |
| **Person being maintained by the deceased** | Eligible — must prove immediate dependency at time of death |

**Grounds**: The will or intestacy rules do not make "reasonable financial provision" for the applicant.

**Two standards of provision**:
1. **Spouse standard**: Such provision as would be reasonable in all circumstances (includes lump sum, periodical payments)
2. **Dependant standard**: Such provision as would be reasonable for the applicant's maintenance (not enrichment — must be needed for support)

**Court's considerations** (s.5, Cap 481):
- Financial resources and needs of the applicant (current and foreseeable)
- Financial resources and needs of other beneficiaries
- Size and nature of the estate
- Obligations and responsibilities of the deceased toward the applicant and others
- Physical or mental disability of the applicant or any beneficiary
- Conduct of the applicant and others (only if relevant)
- Age of the applicant and duration of the marriage (spouses)
- Contribution made by the applicant to the welfare of the family (non-financial contributions)

**Orders the court may make** (s.6, Cap 481):
- Periodical payments (secured or unsecured)
- Lump sum payment (immediate or by instalments)
- Transfer of property
- Settlement of property
- Variation of any settlement made under the will or intestacy
- Variation of a trust

**Time limit**: Application must be made within **6 months** from the date of the grant of representation (s.12, Cap 481).

**Laches**: Delay may bar a claim even within the 6-month period.

### 5. Procedure for Contentious Probate

**Pre-action protocol**:
- Letter of claim to executors / propounders of the will
- Exchange of relevant documents
- Consider mediation (strongly encouraged by the court)

**Issue proceedings**:
- **Writ / Originating Summons** (depending on the nature of the claim)
- **Statement of Claim**: Full particulars of the grounds of challenge
- **Defence**: Propounder's response
- **Counterclaim**: Propounder may counterclaim for probate in solemn form

**Case management**:
- Case management conference
- Directions for disclosure, witness statements, expert evidence (medical, handwriting, etc.)
- Trial date

**Interim relief**:
- Caveat (Form 25) — prevents grant being issued without notice
- **Standing search**: Request to be notified of any application for grant
- **Interim distribution**: Court may order partial distribution to needy beneficiaries

### 6. Mediation and ADR

**Practice Direction on Mediation in Family/Probate Cases**:
- Court strongly encourages mediation in all estate disputes
- Adverse costs consequences for unreasonable refusal to mediate
- Mediation is particularly suitable for:
  - Family provision claims (compromise possible)
  - Disputes over will construction (non-contentious)
  - Disagreements between co-executors
- Less suitable for: forgery, capacity where there is clear medical evidence

**Costs consequences**: The court will consider which party unreasonably refused or failed to engage in ADR when making costs orders.

### 7. Costs in Probate Litigation

**General rule**: Costs follow the event (loser pays)

**Exceptions**:
- **Probate special rule**: If the testator's conduct or the surrounding circumstances caused the litigation (e.g., the will was ambiguous or the testator acted suspiciously), costs may be paid from the estate
- **Beddoe application**: Trustee/executor may apply to court for directions on whether to litigate — if approved, costs come from the estate regardless of outcome
- **Calderbank offer / formal offer**: Formal offer to settle (Petersen v Petersen) — if an offeree fails to beat the offer at trial, costs consequences follow

### 8. Settlement and Compromise

**Methods of settlement**:
- **Consent order**: Parties agree and court approves (court must be satisfied the compromise is for the benefit of all interested parties, especially minors and unborns)
- **Tomlin order**: Stay of proceedings with agreed terms scheduled
- **Deed of family arrangement**: Variation of the will or intestacy by agreement — must involve all interested parties; if minors are involved, court approval may be needed

**Effect of settlement**:
- Binding on all parties who agreed
- If the estate has already been granted, the grant remains valid but the distribution is varied
- IRD clearance may need to be re-issued if the variation affects stamp duty or tax

## Common Pitfalls

- **Late claims**: Cap 481 claims must be within 6 months of the grant — time is short
- **No medical evidence**: Without contemporaneous medical notes, capacity challenges are very difficult
- **Unrealistic expectations**: Family provision claims rarely result in disinheriting the primary beneficiary — they seek reasonable provision, not equality
- **Costs exposure**: Will disputes are expensive — costs can easily exceed the value of the estate
- **Misunderstanding "reasonable provision"**: For dependants (non-spouse), the standard is maintenance, not enrichment
- **Statutory wills**: Consider the Mental Health Ordinance Cap 136 — the court can make a statutory will for a person who lacks testamentary capacity
