# Estate Administration — Skill

## Purpose

Guide executors and administrators through the full process of administering a deceased person's estate in Hong Kong, from the grant of representation through to final distribution and discharge, under the Probate and Administration Ordinance (Cap 10).

## Scope

- Executor and administrator duties
- Collecting and valuing assets
- Paying debts and expenses
- Distribution to beneficiaries
- Estate accounts and reporting
- IRD clearance and stamp duty
- Cross-border administration
- Contested administration issues

## Workflow

### 1. Immediate Steps After Death (First 2 Weeks)

**Urgent actions**:
- Obtain certified copies of death certificate (minimum 10 copies)
- Locate the original will and notify executors
- Secure the deceased's property and valuables
- Notify banks, employers, pension providers, insurance companies
- Cancel credit cards, direct debits, standing orders
- Notify the Land Registry if property is involved
- Inform the Immigration Department (if deceased was a foreign national)
- Arrange funeral (note: executors can pay reasonable funeral expenses from the estate even before grant)

**Documents to gather**:
- Passport, HKID card
- Marriage certificate (if applicable)
- Property deeds and Land Registry search
- Bank statements (all accounts, last 12 months)
- Share certificates and broker statements
- Insurance policies (life, property, travel)
- MPF scheme statements
- Loan and credit agreements
- Tax returns (last 7 years)
- Employment contract and benefits
- Previous wills and codicils

### 2. Valuing the Estate

**Assets to include**:
- Real property in HK (get valuation from a professional valuer)
- Bank accounts (balance at date of death, plus accrued interest)
- Shares and investments (value at date of death)
- MPF (lump sum death benefit, paid to nominated beneficiary or estate)
- Insurance policies (if payable to the estate, not to a named beneficiary)
- Vehicles, boats (valuation from surveyor)
- Personal chattels (furniture, jewellery, art — professional valuation if valuable)
- Business interests (partnership, sole proprietorship, shares in private companies)
- Intellectual property and royalties
- Digital assets (cryptocurrency, domain names, social media accounts with monetary value)
- Overseas assets (separate valuation in local currency)

**Assets NOT to include**:
- Joint tenancy property (passes by survivorship)
- Life insurance policies with named beneficiaries
- MPF nominated to a specific beneficiary
- Assets held in trust (not beneficially owned by the deceased)

**Liabilities to deduct**:
- Mortgages and secured loans
- Credit card debts and unsecured loans
- Outstanding bills (utilities, management fees, tax)
- Funeral expenses
- Administration expenses (legal, probate, valuation fees)

**Net estate**: Total assets minus liabilities — this is the distributable estate

### 3. Apply for Grant (See Probate Application Skill)

- Obtain grant of probate or letters of administration
- Expected timeline: 4–8 weeks

### 4. Collect Assets (Using the Grant)

**Procedure for each asset type**:

| Asset Type | Action |
|------------|--------|
| **Bank accounts** | Present sealed grant and ID to close accounts; transfer balance to executor's estate account |
| **Shares** | Present grant to share registrar; re-register into executor's name; sell or transfer to beneficiaries |
| **Property** | Present grant to Land Registry; assent to beneficiaries or sell |
| **MPF** | Submit MPF claim form to trustee; MPF paid to estate (unless nominated beneficiary) |
| **Insurance** | Submit claim with death certificate and grant; proceeds to estate (unless named beneficiary) |
| **Debt collection** | If debts are owed to the deceased, demand payment; sue if necessary |

**Estate bank account**: Open a dedicated estate bank account to receive all estate funds — do not mix with personal accounts

### 5. Pay Debts and Expenses

**Statutory priority for payment (s.55 Cap 10)**:
1. Funeral expenses (reasonable)
2. Testamentary and administration expenses (legal fees, probate fees, valuation fees)
3. Secured debts (mortgage, car loan — paid from the secured asset first)
4. Preferential debts (wages, MPF contributions, taxes — within limits)
5. Ordinary debts (credit cards, unsecured loans, utilities, management fees)

**Creditor claims**:
- Advertise for claims (Gazette and Chinese newspaper) — gives creditors 1 month to submit
- After that, pay proven claims
- Executor should not distribute until satisfied no creditors are outstanding
- If uncertain: set aside a reserve fund for contingent claims

### 6. IRD Clearance and Tax

**Stamp duty** (obligation before distribution):
- On HK property: stamp duty on assent or sale (rates depend on property value and buyer status)
- On HK shares: 0.2% (0.1% each for buyer and seller)

**IRD clearance**:
- Lodge estate accounts with IRD
- Obtain IRD certificate confirming no outstanding tax liability
- Distribution before IRD clearance = personal liability for executor

**Deceased's final tax return**:
- File profits tax return (if deceased was in business)
- File salaries tax return (for employment income up to date of death)
- Property tax return (if rental income)
- File within the normal tax filing deadlines

### 7. Distribution to Beneficiaries

**After IRD clearance and payment of all debts**:

**Steps**:
1. Prepare estate accounts for approval by beneficiaries (optional but recommended)
2. Obtain beneficiaries' receipts (formal discharge)
3. Distribute according to the will or intestacy rules

**Distribution types**:
- **Specific legacies**: Transfer the specific asset to the named beneficiary
- **Pecuniary legacies**: Pay the cash amount to the beneficiary
- **Residuary legacies**: Divide the residue according to the will or intestacy shares

**Intestacy distribution (Cap 73)**:
- Spouse only: whole estate to spouse
- Spouse + children: spouse gets HK$500,000 + ½ residue; children get ½ residue equally
- Children only: all to children equally
- Parents only (no spouse/children): all to parents equally
- Siblings only (no spouse/children/parents): all to siblings equally
- No family: estate passes to Government (bona vacantia)

### 8. Estate Accounts (Final Accounting)

**Content of estate accounts**:
1. Statement of assets at date of death (with values)
2. Income received during administration (interest, dividends)
3. Capital gains/losses from asset realisations
4. Payments out (debts, expenses, legacies)
5. Distribution to beneficiaries
6. Balance on completion

**Beneficiary rights**:
- Beneficiaries are entitled to full estate accounts
- Executor must provide accounts promptly
- Beneficiaries may challenge accounts (misfeasance, excessive fees, improper distributions)
- Executor may be required to account for estate property

**Discharge**: Once beneficiaries approve the accounts and sign a receipt, the executor is discharged from further liability (except for fraud or wilful default).

### 9. Final Steps

- Close the estate bank account
- Notify relevant authorities (Land Registry if property transferred)
- Retain estate records for at least 6 years (longer if tax issues may arise)
- Executor's discharge: obtain formal discharge from beneficiaries

### 10. Contentious Administration

**Executor disputes**:
- Removal of executor (court application — s.34 Cap 10)
- Executor refusing to act (renunciation or court order)
- Executor misconduct (breach of duty, misappropriation)

**Beneficiary disputes**:
- Application for directions (executor may apply to court for guidance)
- Beneficiary's claim for family provision (Cap 481)
- Challenge to executor's accounts

## Common Pitfalls

- **Distributing before IRD clearance**: Executor becomes personally liable for unpaid tax
- **Paying debts in wrong order**: Non-preferential creditors must not be paid before preferential ones
- **Failure to advertise for creditors**: Executor may be liable for missed claims
- **Selling assets below market value**: Breach of duty — must obtain best price
- **Delegating administration**: Executor remains responsible for acts of agents
- **Ignoring proprietary estoppel claims**: A potential claim by a person promised an interest in the estate
- **Cross-border assets**: Administration can be significantly more complex — seek local advice
