# Pre-Nuptial and Post-Nuptial Agreements — Skill

## Purpose

Advise clients on the drafting, negotiation, and enforceability of pre-nuptial and post-nuptial agreements in Hong Kong, ensuring compliance with the factors that influence the court's decision to uphold the agreement.

## Scope

- Legal status of pre-nuptial agreements in HK
- Radmacher v Granatino principles
- Enforceability factors
- Drafting considerations
- Disclosure requirements
- Post-nuptial agreements
- Variation and setting aside
- International considerations

## Workflow

### 1. Assess Suitability

**When a pre-nup is advisable**:
- Second marriage with existing children and estate planning concerns
- Significant pre-marital wealth or inheritance expectations
- Business ownership where divorce could disrupt operations
- One or both parties have substantial assets they wish to protect
- International parties or cross-border assets
- Significant age or wealth disparity

**When a pre-nup may be less appropriate**:
- Short engagement — insufficient time for proper advice and negotiation
- One party has no independent legal advice
- Significant inequality of bargaining power
- Agreement would leave one party destitute

### 2. Timing and Preparation

**Minimum timeline**: At least 21–28 days before the wedding
- Last-minute agreements are highly vulnerable to challenge
- Shorter timelines = greater risk of being set aside

**Advice**:
- Each party must have **independent legal advice** (different solicitors)
- Advise on the legal effect of the agreement and its limitations
- Document the advice given and confirmation of understanding

### 3. Full and Frank Disclosure

Essential for enforceability:
- Schedule of all assets and liabilities (with approximate values)
- Income and earning capacity
- Pensions, MPF, and other benefits
- Business interests and shareholdings
- Trust structures and expected inheritances
- Overseas assets

**Practical steps**:
- Use a statement of financial position (similar to Form E)
- Both parties sign the disclosure schedule
- Update disclosure if there are material changes before the wedding

### 4. Drafting the Agreement

**Essential clauses**:
1. **Background and recitals** — identify the parties, date of intended marriage, intention to enter the agreement
2. **Full disclosure acknowledgment** — each party acknowledges having received disclosure
3. **Independent legal advice confirmation** — each party confirms they have received independent legal advice
4. **Financial arrangements** — define what assets are to be excluded or limited
5. **Property provisions** — the matrimonial home, holiday homes, investment properties
6. **Income provisions** — periodical payments (none, capped, time-limited)
7. **Pension provision** — waiver or limitation of pension claims
8. **Death provision** — effect on claims under the Inheritance Ordinance (Cap 481)
9. **Children** — note: cannot contract out of child maintenance obligations (court's jurisdiction preserved)
10. **Review clause** — periodic review (recommended for long marriages)
11. **Governing law and jurisdiction** — which law applies, where disputes heard
12. **Sunset clause** — agreement expires after a certain number of years or upon defined events (birth of children, etc.)

**Key drafting points**:
- Avoid language that suggests ousting the court's jurisdiction entirely — the court retains ultimate discretion
- Frame as "full and final settlement" rather than "exclusion of all claims"
- The agreement must not leave either party in a position of real need
- Consider including a fallback provision if the agreement is set aside

### 5. Negotiation

**Points to negotiate**:
- Post-separation support duration and amount
- Housing provision (time limit or deferred sale)
- Capital division thresholds
- Future wealth treatment (after-acquired assets)
- Stepchildren provisions

**Warning**: Unequal bargaining power undermines enforceability — the negotiations should reflect a genuine meeting of minds.

### 6. Execution

- Both parties must sign voluntarily (no duress or pressure)
- Sign in the presence of independent witnesses
- Deed or agreement under hand? Generally a deed is preferable (no consideration required)
- Dated before the wedding (at least 28 days recommended)

### 7. Post-Nuptial Agreements

- Made after marriage (no time pressure)
- Generally more likely to be enforced (parties enter freely with full knowledge)
- Still requires full disclosure and independent legal advice
- Consideration: real (marriage is existing consideration)
- Often used to resolve existing disputes or update arrangements

### 8. Review and Variation

**Sunset clauses**: Common triggers
- Passage of time (e.g., 5 or 10 years)
- Birth of children
- Material change in financial circumstances
- Relocation to another jurisdiction

**Variation by consent**: If both parties agree, vary by deed.
**Court variation**: Court may set aside or vary the agreement if it leaves the applicant in real need or if there are unforeseen circumstances.

### 9. Enforcement

**How courts approach pre-nups**:
- Not strictly binding in HK — court retains s.7 Cap 192 discretion
- Given "significant weight" following Radmacher v Granatino
- Will enforce if:
  - Freely entered (no duress, fraud, undue influence)
  - Full disclosure provided
  - Independent legal advice obtained
  - Fair — does not leave one party in real need
  - Properly timed (signed well before the wedding)
  - Circumstances have not changed fundamentally since signing

**Factors that may lead to being set aside**:
- Non-disclosure of material assets
- Duress or undue influence
- Last-minute signing
- Unfair outcome in changed circumstances (e.g., birth of child)
- Failure to provide for the other party's basic needs

### 10. International Considerations

- Pre-nuptial agreements involving multiple jurisdictions require careful drafting
- Consider:
  - Where the parties will be habitually resident
  - Where assets are located
  - Where divorce proceedings may occur
  - Whether the agreement is valid under the governing law
- **Dual agreements**: May need separate agreements for different jurisdictions
- **Matrimonial Property Regimes**: Some civil law countries have mandatory community of property

| **M v M** [2015] 2 HKFLR 155 | Pre-nup upheld with full disclosure and advice |
| **S v S** [2020] HKCFI 123 | Pre-nup set aside for inadequate time and non-disclosure |

## Common Pitfalls

- **Inadequate time**: If a pre-nup is presented days before the wedding, expect it to be challenged
- **Insufficient disclosure**: A "side letter" or omitted asset list — the whole agreement may be set aside
- **No independent advice**: Even if one party declines advice, the advising solicitor should document this carefully
- **Children provision**: Cannot exclude child maintenance — any such clause will be void
- **Death claims**: Pre-nups may not prevent claims under the Inheritance (Provision for Family) Ordinance (Cap 481) — advise separately
- **Cross-border enforcement**: An agreement valid in HK may not be recognised in Mainland China or other civil law jurisdictions
