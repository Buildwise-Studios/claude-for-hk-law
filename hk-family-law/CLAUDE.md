# HK Family Law Practice Profile

## Practice Area Overview

Hong Kong family law derives from English common law and is codified primarily through several key ordinances. The District Court has jurisdiction over most family proceedings under the **District Court Ordinance (Cap 336)**, while the Court of First Instance handles more complex or high-value cases.

The welfare of the child is the paramount consideration in all proceedings concerning children. Mediation is strongly encouraged by the court (PD 15.1–15.5), and legal aid is available through the Legal Aid Department.

## Key Legislation

| Legislation | Key Provisions |
|-------------|----------------|
| **Matrimonial Causes Ordinance (Cap 179)** | Divorce, judicial separation, nullity of marriage |
| **Matrimonial Proceedings and Property Ordinance (Cap 192)** | Financial relief, property adjustment, pension sharing |
| **Guardianship of Minors Ordinance (Cap 13)** | Custody, guardianship, access, maintenance for children |
| **Separation and Maintenance Orders Ordinance (Cap 16)** | Financial provision for spouses without divorce |
| **Marriage Ordinance (Cap 181)** | Solemnisation of marriages, validity |
| **Matrimonial Causes Rules (Cap 179A)** | Procedural rules for divorce and ancillary relief |
| **Parent and Child Ordinance (Cap 429)** | Parentage, DNA testing, parental responsibility |
| **Adoption Ordinance (Cap 290)** | Domestic and intercountry adoption |
| **Domestic Violence Ordinance (Cap 189)** | Protection against domestic violence (injunctions, arrest powers) |
| **Child Abduction and Custody Ordinance (Cap 512)** | Implementation of the Hague Convention on child abduction |

## Divorce under Cap 179

### Ground for Divorce

Section 11 of Cap 179 provides the sole ground for divorce: **irretrievable breakdown of the marriage**, established by proving one or more of five facts:

1. **Adultery** — the respondent has committed adultery and the petitioner finds it intolerable to live with the respondent (s.11A)
2. **Unreasonable behaviour** — the respondent has behaved in such a way that the petitioner cannot reasonably be expected to live with the respondent (s.11B)
3. **Desertion** — the respondent has deserted the petitioner for a continuous period of at least two years immediately before the petition (s.11C)
4. **Two years' separation with consent** — the parties have lived apart for a continuous period of at least two years and the respondent consents to the decree (s.11D)
5. **Five years' separation** — the parties have lived apart for a continuous period of at least five years immediately before the petition (s.11E)

### The One-Year Bar

Section 12 prohibits divorce petitions within the first year of marriage except in exceptional circumstances involving exceptional hardship or depravity. This is strictly applied.

### Procedure

1. **Petition** — filed with the Family Court (District Court) or CFI (for high-value cases)
2. **Acknowledgement of Service** — respondent must file within 8 days (HK) or 21 days (overseas)
3. **Answer** — respondent may file an answer within 14 days
4. **Undefended -> Special Procedure** — majority of cases; decree nisi granted on affidavit evidence
5. **Decree Nisi** — provisional order; parties must wait at least 6 weeks before applying for decree absolute
6. **Decree Absolute** — final dissolution of marriage

### Judicial Separation

Available under s.24 of Cap 179 where divorce grounds exist but one party objects on religious or other grounds. Does not dissolve the marriage but relieves parties of cohabitation obligations.

### Nullity

- **Void marriages** — bigamy, prohibited degrees, non-compliance with formalities (s.20)
- **Voidable marriages** — non-consummation, lack of consent, mental disorder, venereal disease, pregnancy by another (s.21)
- Bars to nullity: approbation, time limits, knowledge (s.24A)

## Financial Relief under Cap 192

### Court's Powers

The District Court and CFI have broad discretion under s.5–12 of Cap 192:

| Type of Order | Description |
|--------------|-------------|
| **Periodical payments** (s.5) | Ongoing maintenance (secured or unsecured) |
| **Lump sum order** (s.6) | Single payment or instalments |
| **Property adjustment** (s.6–7) | Transfer of property, settlement, variation of settlement |
| **Pension sharing / attachment** (s.7A–7D) | Division of pension rights (MPF and other schemes) |
| **Sale of property** (s.8) | Order for sale with division of proceeds |
| **Variation** (s.11) | Variation of periodical payments, settlements |

### Statutory Factors (s.7, Cap 192)

The court considers:

1. **Income, earning capacity, property** and other financial resources of the parties
2. **Financial needs, obligations** and responsibilities of the parties
3. **Standard of living** enjoyed before the breakdown
4. **Age** of the parties and duration of the marriage
5. **Physical or mental disability** of either party
6. **Contributions** — financial and non-financial (including care of the family and home)
7. **Conduct** — only if it would be inequitable to disregard
8. **Loss of benefit** (pension, other rights) on dissolution

### Principles of Distribution

Hong Kong courts follow the **three-stage approach** from the English case law (*White v White*, *Miller v Miller*):

1. **Needs** — financial needs of each party (primary consideration)
2. **Sharing** — equal sharing of matrimonial assets (long marriages)
3. **Compensation** — for economic disadvantage arising from the marriage

### Matrimonial vs Non-Matrimonial Property

- **Matrimonial property** — acquired during the marriage and for the family — generally shared
- **Non-matrimonial property** — pre-marital assets, inheritances, gifts from third parties — may be excluded or given less weight
- The distinction is not rigid; the court has discretion

### Maintenance Pending Suit (s.3)

Interim maintenance orders during the proceedings — typically calculated on a "without prejudice" budget basis.

### Clean Break

Courts favour a clean break — a final settlement with no continuing financial obligations. This is achieved through:

- Lump sum payment in lieu of periodical payments
- Property transfer or settlement
- Dismissal of the other party's claims on remarriage or on order

## Child Arrangements

### Parental Responsibility

Under the **Parent and Child Ordinance (Cap 429)** and **Guardianship of Minors Ordinance (Cap 13)**:

- Both parents have equal parental responsibility for a child
- Parental responsibility is not lost on divorce or separation
- Custody and access are determined by the court

### Welfare Principle (s.3, Cap 13)

The **welfare of the child is the paramount consideration** in all proceedings concerning the upbringing of a child. This includes:

- Physical and emotional well-being
- Educational needs
- Maintaining relationships with both parents (where safe)
- The child's wishes (depending on age and maturity)
- Harm prevention
- Capacity of each parent to meet the child's needs

### Custody and Access

- **Sole custody** — one parent has day-to-day care and control
- **Joint custody** — both parents share major decision-making; one may have primary care and control
- **Access / contact** — defined arrangements for the non-resident parent
- **Specific issue orders** — court determines specific questions (education, medical treatment, relocation)

### Relocation (Leave to Remove)

- Removal of a child from HK requires either the other parent's consent or the court's permission
- Governing principle: welfare of the child
- Key factors: motive of the applicant parent, practicality of maintaining contact, child's connection to both HK and proposed destination, impact on the child
- Leading HK case: **PD v LKW** (relocation principles)

### Child Maintenance

- Under Cap 16 and Cap 13 — both parents have a duty to maintain their children
- Applied via the **Child Maintenance Guidelines** — published by the Judiciary
- Guidelines recommend a percentage of income based on number of children:
  - 1 child: 20% of net income
  - 2 children: 30% of net income
  - 3+ children: 40% of net income
- Court has discretion to depart from guidelines
- Maintenance payable until age 18 (or 21 if in education)

## Mediation and ADR

### Practice Direction 15.1–15.5

The HK Judiciary strongly encourages mediation in family disputes:

- **PD 15.1** — parties must consider mediation before proceedings
- **PD 15.2** — voluntary but court will consider refusal in costs
- **PD 15.3** — financial relief cases specifically
- **PD 15.4** — children cases
- **PD 15.5** — certification of consideration of mediation required

### Accredited Mediators

Mediation must be conducted by HKMAAL-accredited family mediators.

### Costs Consequences

Unreasonable refusal to mediate may result in adverse costs orders.

## Pre-Nuptial and Post-Nuptial Agreements

### Status under HK Law

Pre-nuptial agreements are **not strictly binding** in HK but are **given significant weight** following the English landmark case *Radmacher v Granatino* [2010] UKSC 42.

### Enforceability Factors

For a pre-nup to be enforced by the court:

1. **Freely entered** — no duress, fraud, undue influence
2. **Full disclosure** — both parties must have disclosed their assets
3. **Independent legal advice** — each party should have separate legal advice
4. **Fairness** — the agreement must not leave one party in a position of real need
5. **Timing** — must be signed at least 21–28 days before the wedding
6. **No vitiating factors** — no misrepresentation, mistake, unconscionability

### Post-Nuptial Agreements

Same principles apply but generally more likely to be upheld as they are made after marriage (less pressure).

## Domestic Violence

### Domestic Violence Ordinance (Cap 189)

- **Protection orders** — Non-molestation orders, exclusion orders
- **Arrest powers** — power of arrest attached where violence has been used or threatened
- **Co-habiting** — covers married and unmarried couples living together
- **Emergency protection** — ex parte applications available in urgent cases
- **Breach** — criminal offence, punishable by fine and imprisonment

## Cross-Border Family Law

### Mainland-HK Divorce Recognition

- The **Arrangement on Reciprocal Recognition and Enforcement of Judgments in Matrimonial and Family Cases** (2022) permits mutual recognition of dissolution judgments between HK and Mainland China
- Financial relief and child arrangements are **not** covered by the arrangement
- Parties should seek enforcement of ancillary orders in the Mainland separately

### Hague Convention on Child Abduction

- **Child Abduction and Custody Ordinance (Cap 512)** implements the Hague Convention
- Applies to contracting states only (not Mainland China)
- Purpose: prompt return of wrongfully removed children
- Central Authority for HK: Director of Social Welfare

### Jurisdictional Issues

- HK courts have jurisdiction if either party is **domiciled** in HK or has **habitual residence** in HK for at least one year immediately before the petition
- For children: HK courts have jurisdiction if the child is **habitually resident** in HK

## Adoption

### Adoption Ordinance (Cap 290)

- Domestic and intercountry adoption governed by the Director of Social Welfare
- Court order vests parental rights and duties in the adoptive parent(s)
- Child's welfare is the paramount consideration
- Adoption orders may be made for children under 18
- Consent of birth parents required unless dispensed with by the court

## Practice Guidance

When advising on family matters:

1. Always assess the **one-year bar** (s.12 Cap 179) before filing a divorce petition
2. For financial relief, prepare a **schedule of assets** early — both matrimonial and non-matrimonial
3. In child cases, the **welfare principle** is paramount — evidence of the child's best interests must be the focus
4. **Mediation** should be suggested at every stage — document any refusal carefully as it may affect costs
5. For pre-nups, ensure **full disclosure and independent legal advice** — the agreement must not leave either party destitute
6. In cross-border cases, check **jurisdiction** first — dual proceedings in HK and the Mainland are possible
7. Always check the **Child Maintenance Guidelines** for the correct percentage range
8. For high-net-worth cases, consider filing in the CFI rather than the Family Court
