# HK Property & Conveyancing Practice Profile

## Practice Area Overview

Hong Kong property law is governed primarily by the **Conveyancing and Property Ordinance (Cap 219)**, the **Land Registration Ordinance (Cap 128)**, and the **Building Management Ordinance (Cap 344)**. Hong Kong's land system is unique — all land is held under government leaseholds (no freehold), and the Land Registry operates a deeds registration system rather than a Torrens title system.

New Territories land adds complexity through the **New Territories Ordinance (Cap 97)** and the application of customary Chinese customary law to certain land transactions.

## Key Legislation

| Legislation | Key Provisions |
|-------------|----------------|
| **Conveyancing and Property Ordinance (Cap 219)** | Sale and purchase, leases, mortgages, covenants, easements, co-ownership, deeds |
| **Land Registration Ordinance (Cap 128)** | Registration of deeds, priority, caveats, pending actions, bankruptcy orders |
| **Building Management Ordinance (Cap 344)** | Deed of Mutual Covenant (DMC), Incorporated Owners (IO), management committees, building disputes |
| **Stamp Duty Ordinance (Cap 117)** | Ad valorem stamp duty, Buyer's Stamp Duty (BSD), Special Stamp Duty (SSD), new residential stamp duty regimes |
| **Landlord and Tenant (Consolidation) Ordinance (Cap 7)** | Residential and commercial tenancies, security of tenure, rent control (limited) |
| **New Territories Ordinance (Cap 97)** | NT land, customary Chinese custom, ancestral land, village houses, Small House Policy |
| **Rating Ordinance (Cap 116)** | Rates on properties, government rent, valuation |
| **Government Rent and Premium (Apportionment) Ordinance (Cap 125)** | Government rent on land grants |
| **Town Planning Ordinance (Cap 131)** | Zoning, planning permissions, enforcement, appeals |
| **Estate Duty Ordinance (Cap 111)** (repealed) | Historical relevance only |

## Land Tenure System

### Government Leases
- All land in Hong Kong is held under Crown and later Government leases
- Private freehold does not exist (except for St John's Cathedral)
- Government leases run for 50, 75, or 999 years (depending on grant date)
- **Initial lease**: Sold at auction (premium paid)
- **Government rent**: Annual rent (3% of rateable value since the Joint Declaration amendments)
- **Conditions of grant**: Building covenants, user restrictions, assignment limitations
- **Extension**: Leases extended under the Joint Declaration provisions and the Extension of Government Leases Ordinance (Cap 1506)

### Types of Land
- **Urban land** — Hong Kong Island, Kowloon, New Kowloon (old Schedule lands)
- **New Territories land** — governed by NT Ordinance (Cap 97), customary Chinese custom applies
- **Village lands** — Small House Policy, ancestral land, NT exempted houses

## Conveyancing Process

### Stages of a Sale and Purchase Transaction

1. **Preliminary** — Agreement for Sale and Purchase (ASP), deposit (typically 5–10%), conditions precedent (mortgage approval, building inspection)
2. **Due Diligence** — Land Registry searches, building plans, DMC review, title investigation
3. **Formal Contract** — Formal sale and purchase agreement, further deposit (to bring total to 10–20%)
4. **Pre-completion** — Mortgage arrangement, requisitions on title, assignment preparation
5. **Completion** — Payment of balance, assignment of property, release of deposit, Land Registry registration

### Key Documents
- **Provisional Sale and Purchase Agreement** — initial binding agreement typically through an estate agent
- **Formal Sale and Purchase Agreement** — detailed terms, usually within 14 days of provisional
- **Assignment** — deed transferring legal title
- **Memorial** — registered memorial at Land Registry
- **Deed of Mutual Covenant (DMC)** — governs building management (residential developments)
- **Charge / Mortgage** — security document for bank financing
- **Licence to Assign** — consent to assign from mortgagee (bank)

### Title Investigation
- Check **Government lease**: term, conditions of grant, user restrictions, building covenant
- Check **Deeds of Mutual Covenant**: management rights, car park ownership, common area sharing
- Check **Register of land**: search for pending actions, orders, caveats, charges, deeds
- Check **User/Use restrictions**: planning permission, deed restrictions
- Verify **Identity of registered owner**: ensure chain of title is continuous
- Check **Building approvals**: occupation permit (OP), building plans, unauthorized building works (UBWs)
- **Title deeds should be examined for at least 15 years** (or from the root of title if longer)

### Common Issues
- Unauthorized building works (can be requisitioned)
- Sub-standard DMCs
- Missing/destroyed title deeds (statutory declaration route)
- New Territories land complications
- Government lease conditions (especially building covenants)
- Pre-emption rights under DMC
- Mortgages requiring discharge before assignment

## Co-ownership

### Types of Co-ownership
- **Joint tenancy** — right of survivorship (jus accrescendi), each owns whole, no individual share
- **Tenancy in common** — each owns an undivided share, no survivorship, distinct ownership rights

### Severance
- Joint tenancy may be severed by:
  - Notice in writing (service on other joint tenants)
  - Act of any joint tenant operating on own share
  - Mutual agreement
  - Bankruptcy of a joint tenant
  - Murder of one joint tenant by another

### Rights of Co-owners
- Occupation and use (proportionate)
- Sale of the property (all must join unless severance/partition)
- Partition — court order for physical division or sale (s.12 Cap 219)
- Contribution to outgoings (rates, repairs, management fees)
- Accounting for rent received from third parties

## Easements and Restrictive Covenants

### Easements (s.24-33 Cap 219)
- **Positive easements**: right of way, right of drainage, right to light, right of support
- **Creation**: express grant, reservation, implied grant (necessity, common intention), prescription
- **Registration**: must be registered under Cap 128 to bind successors

### Restrictive Covenants
- Burden on land (negative in nature)
- Benefit may pass with dominant tenement
- Enforcement: only dominant owner can enforce
- Discharge: by court order, unification of titles, lapse of time (rare), variation by Land Tribunal
- Binding successors: rules in equity (Tulk v Moxhay), confirmed in s.41 Cap 219

### Party Walls
- No specific Party Wall Act in HK — common law principles apply
- Statutory provisions in Cap 219 (s.26-27) for shared walls, support, and drainage

## Mortgages

### Types of Mortgages
- **Legal mortgage** (charge by way of legal mortgage under Cap 219)
- **Equitable mortgage** (deposit of title deeds)
- **Second / subsequent mortgages** (ranking determined by registration priority)

### Rights of Mortgagee
- Possession — physical possession and/or receipt of rent
- Power of sale — after default, subject to 1 month's notice (s.50-54 Cap 219)
- Foreclosure — rare in HK, court order extinguishing equity of redemption
- Appointment of receiver

### Rights of Mortgagor
- Equity of redemption — right to redeem on payment
- Right to inspect deeds
- Right to lease (subject to mortgagee's consent)

## Tenancies (Residential and Commercial)

### Residential Tenancies
- Regulated under Part IV of the **Landlord and Tenant (Consolidation) Ordinance (Cap 7)** (limited rent control)
- **Fixed term tenancy**: 2 years standard, contractual arrangement
- **Periodic tenancy**: monthly or quarterly rollover, notice period (ss.94-99, Cap 7)
- Security of tenure: limited protection; Part II (domestic tenancies) applies only to pre-1981 buildings
- Standard terms: rent, management fee, rates, government rent, deposit (typically 2 months), repair obligations

### Commercial Tenancies
- Governed by Part V of Cap 7 (security of tenure for shops) — repealed for new tenancies
- Most commercial tenancies are contractual (no statutory security of tenure)
- Key terms: rent review mechanism, assignment/sub-letting clauses, fit-out period, option to renew

### Landlord Obligations
- Quiet enjoyment (implied covenant)
- Repair — structural repairs usually on landlord (s.5 Cap 7 for domestic tenancies)
- Provide receipt for rent (s.12 Cap 7)
- Repayment of deposit (subject to deduction for damages)

### Tenant Obligations
- Payment of rent and outgoings (rates)
- Repair — internal repairs by tenant (unless otherwise agreed)
- Non-waste; not to assign or sublet without consent
- Yield up possession on determination

## Building Management

### Deed of Mutual Covenant (DMC)
- Governs rights and obligations of owners
- Defines: common areas, management rights, management fees, prohibition on alterations
- Registration at Land Registry required
- **Irrevocable** — cannot be changed without unanimous consent (or court order)
- Areas of focus: car park ownership and rights, clubhouse and facilities, signage, exclusive-use areas

### Incorporated Owners (IO)
- **Formation**: Created under Cap 344 by automatic incorporation on conveyance of 20%+ of shares or by application to Land Tribunal
- **Management Committee**: Elected by owners at annual general meeting
- Powers: collect management fees, enforce DMC, maintain common areas, insure building, engage contractor
- **Duties**: comply with DMC, manage funds prudently, hold AGM, insure building — full fiduciary duties to all owners
- Enforcement powers: sue for arrears, enforce DMC terms, regulate alterations, claim costs from owners

### Building Disputes
- **Land Tribunal — Building Management jurisdiction**:
  - Interpretation of DMC
  - Enforcement of DMC obligations
  - Appointment of administrator (under Cap 344)
  - Appeals from IO decisions
  - Variation of DMC (limited circumstances)
- Common disputes: management fee arrears, unauthorized alterations, misappropriation of funds, election challenges

## Stamp Duty

### Types of Stamp Duty (Cap 117)

| Type | Description | Rate (as of latest revision) |
|------|-------------|------|
| **Ad Valorem Stamp Duty (AVD)** | Standard rate on property transfers | Scale rates (variable depending on property value) |
| **Buyer's Stamp Duty (BSD)** | Additional duty on non-HK permanent resident buyers and companies purchasing residential property | 7.5% on consideration or market value |
| **Special Stamp Duty (SSD)** | On resale within short holding period (residential only) | 10–20% (sliding scale by holding period) |
| **New Residential Stamp Duty (NRSD)** | Additional duty on HKPRs acquiring additional residential property | 7.5% |

### Key Points
- Stamp duty payable by purchaser
- Time limit: 30 days from date of agreement
- Double stamp duty if time limit missed
- Stamp duty adjudication required for most transactions
- Relief available: intra-group transfers, company reorganisations, family transfers, first-time HKPR buyers
- Market value used where consideration is below market

## New Territories Land

### Special Characteristics
- Governed by New Territories Ordinance (Cap 97)
- Customary Chinese custom applies (traditional Chinese customary land law)
- **Small House Policy**: indigenous male NT villagers may apply for licence to build small house (3 storeys / 700 sq ft)
- **Family arrangements**: ancestral land held by tso/tong (family trusts)
- **Blocked Crown leases**: NT land subject to extended agricultural leases
- **Alienation restrictions**: NT land historically required consents or licences — mostly removed but some remain

### Customary Rights
- Fung shui paths, man made features, graves, orchards, bamboos
- Rights of way over NT agricultural land (permissive user)
- Village access, water rights, drainage

### Due Diligence for NT Land
- Verify Small House licensing status
- Check for ancestral trust interests
- Review NT map and lease conditions
- Confirm no outstanding government development/resumption plans

## Key Case Law

- **Hysan Development Co Ltd v Town Planning Board** — Planning blight and compensation
- **Wong Wing Tsan v Hong Kong Housing Authority** — DMC and management rights
- **Gaywan Investments Ltd v Yung Ho** — Commercial tenancy security of tenure
- **China Field Ltd v Appeal Tribunal (Buildings)** — Building enforcement and unauthorized works
- **PCCW-HKT Telephone Ltd v City Telecom (HK) Ltd** — Easements and restrictive covenants
- **Re Grand Concord Investments Ltd** — Mortgagee's power of sale and duties
- **Wong Ka Yuen v Chairman of Appeal Panel** — Land resumption and compensation

## Practice Guidance

When advising on property matters:
1. Always conduct a full Land Registry search and title investigation — do not rely on seller's solicitors alone
2. Check the Government lease conditions — many contain surprising restrictions (user clauses, building covenants, assignment restrictions)
3. For residential purchases, advise on BSD/SSD/NRSD implications upfront — non-HKPR buyers face significant additional duty
4. For NT land transactions, brief the client on the unique customary law overlay and the Small House Policy
5. In tenancy matters, distinguish between the legal regime for residential (Cap 7 Part IV) and commercial (contractual)
6. For building management disputes, consider mediation before litigation — the Land Tribunal encourages ADR
7. Always register deeds within one month (mandatory under Cap 128) — priority determined by date of registration
8. Watch for anti-money laundering requirements in high-value property transactions
