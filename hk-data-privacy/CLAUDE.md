# HK Data Privacy Practice Profile

## Practice Area Overview

Hong Kong data privacy is governed by the **Personal Data (Privacy) Ordinance (Cap 486)**, enforced by the **Privacy Commissioner for Personal Data (PCPD)**. The PDPO is a principle-based statute structured around **Six Data Protection Principles (DPP1-6)** governing the collection, use, retention, and security of personal data.

The PDPO has been significantly amended, most notably by the **2021 amendments** introducing direct marketing provisions, the **2023 doxxing amendments** creating specific criminal offences (s.64), and enhanced enforcement powers for the PCPD.

## Key Legislation

| Legislation | Key Provisions |
|-------------|----------------|
| **Personal Data (Privacy) Ordinance (Cap 486)** | DPP1-6, data user obligations, data subject rights, direct marketing, doxxing offences, enforcement |
| **PCPD Code of Practice on the Identity Card Number and Other Personal Identifiers** | Collection and use of HKID numbers, biometric data |
| **PCPD Guidance on CCTV and Employee Monitoring** | Surveillance and workplace monitoring |
| **PCPD Guidance on Data Breach Handling** | Voluntary breach notification framework |
| **Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorist Financing Ordinance (Cap 615)** | Customer due diligence, record keeping (overlaps with data privacy) |

| **Personal Data (Privacy) (Amendment) Ordinance 2023** | Doxxing offences (s.64), enhanced PCPD powers |

## The Six Data Protection Principles

### DPP1 — Purpose and Manner of Collection
- Personal data must be collected only for a lawful purpose directly related to the data user's function or activity
- Collection must be necessary and not excessive
- Data subjects must be notified of:
  - Whether collection is mandatory or voluntary
  - The purpose of collection
  - The classes of persons to whom data may be transferred
  - The right to request access and correction
  - The name and address of the person handling access requests
- Collection must be by lawful and fair means

### DPP2 — Accuracy and Retention
- Personal data must be accurate and kept up to date
- Data must not be kept longer than necessary for the purpose
- Data users must take practicable steps to erase data no longer needed
- Retention must be justified by the purpose

### DPP3 — Use of Personal Data
- Personal data must not be used for a new purpose without prescribed consent of the data subject
- "New purpose" means any purpose other than the original collection purpose or a directly related purpose
- Exceptions: prevention of crime, legal proceedings, public health, statistics/research (de-identified), emergency situations
- Data matching and data mining require careful DPP3 analysis

### DPP4 — Security of Personal Data
- Data users must take all practicable steps to ensure personal data is protected against:
  - Unauthorised or accidental access, processing, erasure, loss, or use
- Security measures should include:
  - IT security (encryption, access controls, firewalls, intrusion detection)
  - Physical security (locked cabinets, secure premises, destruction/shredding)
  - Organisational measures (policies, training, access logs, NDAs)
  - Outsourcing — contractual security obligations on data processors

### DPP5 — Information to be Generally Available
- Data users must make available their policies and practices in relation to personal data
- Common forms: Privacy Policy Statement (PPS), Personal Information Collection Statement (PICS)

### DPP6 — Access to Personal Data
- Data subjects have the right to:
  - Request access to personal data held by a data user
  - Request correction of personal data
  - Be provided with a copy of the data (subject to reasonable fee)
- Data users must respond within 40 calendar days (extendable to 50 days where complex)
- Grounds for refusal: limited (security, legal privilege, prejudicing enforcement, third party rights)

## Data Subject Rights

### Access Right (s.18)
- Data subject may request whether data user holds personal data
- Request copy of that data
- Request data user to confirm or deny holding data
- Fee: capped at "reasonable" — PCPD guideline suggests initial fee should not deter exercise of rights

### Correction Right (s.22)
- Data subject may request correction of inaccurate personal data
- Data user must comply or provide reasons for refusal
- If correction made, data user must inform third parties to whom data was disclosed (if practicable)

### Compensation (s.66)
- Data subject who suffers damage (including injury to feelings) as a result of a contravention may claim compensation
- Defence: data user took "reasonably practicable" steps to prevent the contravention
- No cap on damages — courts have awarded significant sums for distress and anxiety

## Direct Marketing (s.35A-J)

### Requirements (introduced 2013, effective 2013)
1. **Must inform** data subject of:
   - Intention to use personal data in direct marketing
   - The classes of goods/services to be marketed
   - Whether the data will be transferred to third parties for their direct marketing
2. **Must obtain consent** (opt-in for use of personal data in direct marketing)
3. **Must obtain written consent** (separate and specific) for transfer of data to third parties for direct marketing
4. **Provide opt-out mechanism** with every marketing communication (free of charge)
5. **Cease use** within 30 days of opt-out request

### Penalties
- Breach of direct marketing provisions: maximum fine of HK$500,000 and imprisonment for 3 years
- Selling personal data for direct marketing without consent: maximum fine of HK$1,000,000 and imprisonment for 5 years

## Doxxing Offences (s.64 — 2023 Amendment)

### Key Provisions
- **s.64(2A)**: Disclosure of personal data without consent with intent to cause harm — maximum fine of HK$100,000 and imprisonment for 2 years
- **s.64(2B)**: Disclosure causing psychological harm — maximum fine of HK$200,000 and imprisonment for 5 years
- **s.64(2C)**: Disclosure causing specified harm (bodily harm, property damage, harassment) — imprisonment for 7 years and unlimited fine
- **s.64(2D)**: Disclosure that gave rise to risk of specified harm — imprisonment for 5 years and fine of HK$1,000,000

### PCPD Enforcement Powers (Enhanced)
- Power to conduct investigations (s.38, 38A)
- Power to issue enforcement notices (s.50)
- Power to demand information (s.38B) — failure without reasonable excuse is a criminal offence
- Power of arrest (s.66L) — for doxxing offences
- Power to apply for search warrants and seize electronic devices (s.66P)
- Power to issue cessation notices to online platforms (s.66ZH)

## Cross-Border Data Transfers

### No Adequacy Decisions
- Hong Kong does not have a system of adequacy decisions like the EU GDPR
- There is no specific restriction on cross-border transfers in the PDPO (unlike China's PIPL or South Korea's PIPA)
- DPP3 applies: transfer outside Hong Kong must be for the original collection purpose (or a directly related purpose) unless consent is obtained

### Contractual Approach
- Data users remain responsible for compliance even when data is processed overseas
- **Recommended contractual measures**:
  - Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with express data protection obligations
  - Restriction on onward transfers
  - Security and confidentiality obligations
  - Notification and cooperation on data breaches
  - Audit and inspection rights
  - Termination rights for breach of data protection obligations
  - Indemnification for PDPO contraventions

### Practical Guidance
- Transfer to related companies within a group: ensure intra-group agreement covers data protection
- Cloud service providers: verify contractual data protection provisions, consider certification (SOC2, ISO 27001)
- Cross-border litigation/discovery: resist blanket data production; seek redaction and data protection orders
- E-discovery: use technology-assisted review to minimise personal data disclosure

## Data Breach Response

### Current Framework (Voluntary, Pre-Legislation)
The PDPO does not currently impose a mandatory data breach notification requirement (though legislative reform is under consultation as of 2024).

### PCPD Guidance on Breach Handling
Recommended steps:
1. **Containment** — Identify the breach, secure the system, preserve evidence
2. **Assessment** — Determine type of data involved, number of affected data subjects, risk of harm
3. **Notification** — Inform PCPD (voluntary but recommended), affected data subjects
4. **Remediation** — Address root cause, improve security measures, implement corrective actions
5. **Review** — Evaluate incident, update policies, conduct staff training

### When to Notify PCPD (Recommended)
- Sensitive personal data involved
- Large volume of data subjects affected
- High risk of identity theft or financial harm
- Systemic failure requiring public attention
- Breach likely to attract media scrutiny

### Regulatory Outcomes
- Investigation by PCPD
- Enforcement notice requiring corrective actions
- Possible criminal prosecution (especially doxxing-related breaches)
- Public reports — significant reputational risk

## Employee Monitoring

### Legal Framework
- No specific legislation governing employee monitoring (unlike GDPR Article 88 or specific EU laws)
- PDPO applies: collection of employee data must be lawful and fair
- PCPD Guidance on Employee Monitoring (2016):
  - Must be for legitimate business purposes
  - Must be proportionate
  - Employees should be informed
  - Covert monitoring only in exceptional circumstances (suspected misconduct, with senior management approval)

### Common Issues
- Email monitoring: inform employees of policy
- Internet usage monitoring: clear acceptable use policy
- CCTV in workplace: must be proportionate, no CCTV in toilets/changing rooms, inform with clear signage
- Location tracking: specific consent recommended (not necessarily legally required)
- Biometric attendance: PCPD recommends alternative (non-biometric) option
- BYOD (Bring Your Own Device): clear policy on device management, data separation, corporate data erasure on termination

## CCTV

### PCPD Code of Practice on Identity Card Number (extends to CCTV)
- Purpose limitation: CCTV only for security and safety
- Proportionality: cameras positioned to minimise capture of unnecessary personal data
- Signage: clear notice of CCTV operation
- Retention: regular deletion cycles, generally not more than 28-60 days
- Disclosure: only to law enforcement on lawful request
- Facial recognition: requires specific privacy impact assessment

## Compliance Programme Design

### Key Components

1. **Data Inventory** — map all personal data flows across the organisation
2. **Privacy Policy Statement (PPS)** — published on website, made available to data subjects
3. **Personal Information Collection Statement (PICS)** — provided at time of collection
4. **Data Retention Policy** — documented retention schedules, secure deletion procedures
5. **Access Request Handling** — designate DPO or access request handler, 40-day response timeline
6. **Direct Marketing Compliance** — consent records, opt-out registry, marketing schedule
7. **Data Breach Response Plan** — written plan, designated response team, external notification template
8. **Data Processor Agreements** — contractual protections with all third-party processors
9. **Staff Training** — annual training on PDPO, data security, breach reporting, direct marketing compliance
10. **Periodic Audit** — internal or external audit of data protection compliance

### Role of the DPO
- Not legally required under PDPO (unlike GDPR or China PIPL)
- Strongly recommended by PCPD as best practice
- Responsibilities: compliance oversight, policy development, incident response, PCPD liaison, training

## Key Case Law

- **Eastweek Publisher Ltd v Privacy Commissioner for Personal Data** — Established principles on DPP1 and DPP3
- **Wong Kwok Wai v Hong Kong Polytechnic University** — Compensation for breach of data privacy
- **Re Ng Ka Chiu and K. v Commissioner of Police** — Disclosure and doxxing offences
- **PCPD v Hong Kong Housing Authority** — Data matching and DPP3 use limitation
- **PCPD v OCTOPUS Cards Ltd** — Enforcement notice and direct marketing compliance
- **Ming Pao Newspapers Ltd v PCPD** — Journalistic activities exemption
- **Re Lau Tung Chun v Hong Kong Broadband Network Ltd** — Data security breach and compensation

## Practice Guidance

When advising on data privacy matters:
1. Assess the data subject's rights timeline carefully — 40-day response (access requests), 30-day cease (direct marketing)
2. For cross-border transfers, prepare contractual DPA provisions as Hong Kong has no adequacy regime
3. Direct marketing requires genuine opt-in consent — pre-ticked boxes or implied consent is insufficient
4. Data breach response is currently voluntary but failure to notify can worsen regulatory outcomes in an investigation
5. Employee monitoring requires careful balancing of business needs with privacy rights
6. The 2023 doxxing amendments create strict criminal liability — ensure clients understand the risks of publishing personal data
7. PCPD enforcement powers have been significantly enhanced — cooperate fully with investigations
8. Privacy Impact Assessments (PIAs) are not legally required but are strongly recommended for high-risk processing
9. Keep abreast of legislative reform — mandatory data breach notification and enhanced cross-border transfer rules are on the horizon
