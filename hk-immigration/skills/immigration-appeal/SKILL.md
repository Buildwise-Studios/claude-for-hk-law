# Immigration Appeal — Skill

## Purpose

Guide legal practitioners through the process of appealing immigration decisions to the Immigration Tribunal under the Immigration Ordinance (Cap 115), including grounds of appeal, procedural requirements, written submissions, oral hearings, and post-appeal options.

## Scope

- Decisions appealable to the Immigration Tribunal
- Time limits for appeal (28 days standard; 14 days for deportation)
- Grounds of appeal
- Written submissions
- Oral hearings
- Tribunal decision and reasons
- Post-appeal options (judicial review)
- Bail pending appeal

## Workflow

### 1. Identify Appealable Decisions

The Immigration Tribunal hears appeals against:

- **Refusal of visa/extension** — GEP, QMAS, TTPS, SLS, dependant visa
- **Refusal of certificate of entitlement** — right of abode claims (CERT)
- **Deportation orders** (s.20–25 Cap 115) — 14-day appeal window
- **Removal orders** (s.53 Cap 115)
- **Refusal of right to land** (s.7/8 Cap 115)
- **Conditions of stay variation** — adverse conditions imposed
- **Refusal of Recognizance Paper cancellation**

**Not appealable to the Tribunal:**
- Administrative decisions (e.g., extension conditions not yet formalised)
- General immigration policy matters
- Decisions of the Chief Executive (may be judicially reviewed)

### 2. Assess Grounds of Appeal

**Procedural grounds:**
- Failure to consider relevant factors
- Reliance on irrelevant factors
- Procedural unfairness (no opportunity to respond to adverse information)
- Failure to give reasons
- Bias or predetermination

**Substantive grounds:**
- Error of fact (misapprehension of key evidence)
- Irrationality (decision no reasonable decision-maker could reach)
- Inconsistent application of published policy
- Failure to apply correct legal test

**Human rights grounds:**
- Breach of BL Art 28 (freedom from torture/inhuman treatment)
- Breach of BL Art 30 (freedom of movement)
- Breach of family/private life rights (Art 8 ECHR principles via BL)
- Non-refoulement obligations under CAT

### 3. Filing the Notice of Appeal

- **Form:** Form ID(E) 1000 (general) or specific form for deportation
- **Time limit:** 28 days from date of decision (14 days for deportation orders)
- **Deadline is strict** — no extension available
- **Where to file:** Immigration Tribunal Secretariat, Immigration Department HQ
- **Contents:** Decision under appeal, grounds, brief facts in support
- **Supporting documents:** Decision letter, representation history, supporting evidence

### 4. Written Submissions

- **Structure:**
  1. Introduction and summary of the case
  2. Chronology of relevant events
  3. The decision under appeal (attach)
  4. Grounds of appeal (expand each ground)
  5. Legal arguments (with case law and legislation)
  6. Submissions on relief sought
  7. Annex: list of supporting evidence

- **Evidence:**
  - Affidavit(s) from the appellant and witnesses
  - Supporting documents (employment records, medical reports, family ties evidence)
  - Character references
  - Legal authorities (case law, policy circulars)

- **Format:**
  - Skeleton arguments (10–15 pages for standard cases)
  - Written submission (up to 30 pages for complex cases)
  - Authorities bundle (indexed, paginated)

### 5. Oral Hearing

- **Discretionary** — Tribunal may decide on papers alone
- **Grounds for requesting oral hearing:**
  - Credibility issues (contested facts)
  - Complex legal arguments requiring elaboration
  - New evidence or change in circumstances
  - Humanitarian factors best addressed in person

- **Hearing structure:**
  - Appellant's submissions (30–60 minutes)
  - Respondent's submissions (Immigration Department representative)
  - Appellant's reply
  - Tribunal questions
  - Decision reserved (typically 2–4 weeks)

### 6. Bail Pending Appeal

- **Application:** To the Immigration Tribunal or CFI
- **Test:** Likelihood of appeal success; risk of flight; public interest considerations
- **Conditions:**
  - Reporting to police/Immigration Department
  - Surrender of travel documents
  - Surety (monetary bond)
  - Residence restriction (curfew, address reporting)
  - Electronic monitoring (in certain cases)

### 7. Tribunal Decision

- **Form:** Written decision with reasons
- **Timing:** Usually 2–4 weeks after hearing (or 4–8 weeks after submission on papers)
- **Outcomes:**
  - **Appeal allowed:** Decision set aside; matter remitted to Director for reconsideration
  - **Appeal dismissed:** Decision affirmed
  - **Vary:** Decision varied (e.g., shorter removal period, different conditions)

### 8. Post-Appeal Options

- **No further statutory appeal** — Tribunal decisions are final
- **Judicial review** (only option):
  - Leave required (O.53 RHC)
  - High threshold: illegality, irrationality, procedural impropriety
  - Grounds must go beyond mere disagreement with the decision
  - Time limit: 3 months from Tribunal decision

## Key Case Law

- **Re an Appellant [2018] HKICM** — Tribunal's approach to credibility findings
- **Secretary for Security v Tang Kwok Wah [2002]** — proportionality analysis
- **Re TT [2020] HKICM** — oral hearing refused, appeal allowed on papers
- **Wong Hau v TCAB [2020] HKCA** — judicial review of Tribunal decisions
- **Re SK [2021] HKICM** — family life and proportionality

## Practice Notes

- **The first 14–28 days are critical** — gather evidence, draft notice, and file within the deadline
- If the deadline has passed, explore judicial review (but note the strict leave application requirements)
- Oral hearings are rarely granted — make a compelling case for why cross-examination or clarification is needed
- Credibility is often the decisive factor — prepare the appellant thoroughly for any hearing
- Human rights arguments should be developed in the Tribunal, not saved for judicial review
- Costs: No costs order in the Tribunal; each party bears its own costs
- Legal aid: Available for deportation appeals (means and merits tested; apply to Director of Legal Aid)
