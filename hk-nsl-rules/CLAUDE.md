## Overview

The **Hong Kong National Security Law (NSL)** was enacted by the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress (NPCSC) on 30 June 2020 and listed in Annex III of the Basic Law. It was promulgated by the Chief Executive for application in the HKSAR on the same day (Cap 599J of the Laws of Hong Kong).

The NSL is a national law applying to Hong Kong under Article 18 of the Basic Law. It overrides inconsistent provisions in Hong Kong domestic legislation. It creates four categories of national security offences, establishes the Committee for Safeguarding National Security, and provides special procedural rules for NSL cases.

## Structure (6 Chapters, 66 Articles)

### Chapter I — General Principles (Arts 1–5)
- **Art 1**: Establishes the NSL's purpose — safeguarding national security and ensuring the "one country, two systems" principle
- **Art 2**: Affirms the HKSAR's obligation to protect national security (reaffirms Basic Law duty)
- **Art 3**: The Central Government holds ultimate responsibility for national security matters
- **Art 4**: Human rights protected — rights and freedoms under the Basic Law, ICESCR, and ICCPR continue to apply (subject to legal restrictions)
- **Art 5**: Rule of law, independent judicial power, presumption of innocence, right to defence

### Chapter II — Offences and Penalties (Arts 20–33)

#### Division 1 — Secession (Art 20)
**Actus reus**: Organising, planning, committing, or participating in acts of secession, including:
- Seceding from the PRC
- Causing the severance of any part of the PRC
- Using force or threat of force to challenge the sovereignty of the PRC in Hong Kong
- Participating in an unlawful assembly or seditious organisation with secessionist intent

**Mens rea**: Intention to commit secession

**Penalty**: 
- Principal offenders: Life imprisonment or fixed-term of 10+ years
- Active participants: 3–10 years
- Other participants: Up to 3 years / detention / disqualification

#### Division 2 — Subversion (Arts 22–23)
**Actus reus**: Organising, planning, committing, or participating in acts of subversion of state power, including:
- Overthrowing or undermining the state's political system
- Attacking or destroying state organs
- Serious interference with the lawful functions of state organs
- Inciting members of the military/Police to mutiny or refuse orders
- Serious disruption of the operation of state organs, LegCo, or the courts

**Mens rea**: Intention to subvert state power

**Penalty**: Same structure as secession (life imprisonment for principals)

#### Division 3 — Terrorism (Arts 24–28)
**Actus reus**: Seriously violent acts endangering national security:
- Assassination, explosions, arson, chemical/biological attacks
- Destruction of transportation, infrastructure, public places
- Hijacking aircraft, ships, or vehicles
- Serious violence against persons or property
- Threats to commit any of the above

**Mens rea**: Intention to seriously endanger national security

**Penalty**: Same structure (life imprisonment for principals)
- Art 27: Organising/participating in terrorist organisations — 3–10 years (principals) / up to 3 years (others)
- Art 28: Providing financial/material support to terrorist organisations — 3–10 years (serious) / up to 3 years or 5 years (depending on culpability)

#### Division 4 — Collusion with Foreign or External Forces (Arts 29–33)
**Actus reus**: Colluding with foreign governments, organisations, or individuals:
- Engaging in or participating in acts endangering national security at the request of foreign forces
- Trespassing or damaging property at the direction of foreign forces
- Intimidating or coercing HK officials with foreign force support
- Inciting HK military/Police mutiny at the instigation of foreign forces
- Requesting foreign interference in HK affairs (e.g. sanctions against HK or PRC)
- Requesting foreign investigation/review of HK affairs

**"Foreign or external forces" defined** (Art 30): Foreign governments, organisations, institutions, individuals, or agencies acting on their behalf

**Mens rea**: Intention to endanger national security through collusion

**Penalty**: Same structure (life imprisonment for principals)

#### Supplementary Offence Provisions (Arts 31–33)
- **Art 31**: Conspiracy, attempt, incitement to commit NSL offences — criminalised
- **Art 32**: Financial and other ancillary liability (forfeiture of proceeds)
- **Art 33**: Mitigation for voluntary disclosure, cooperation, or surrender

### Chapter III — Jurisdiction, Prosecution, and Trial (Arts 34–52)

#### Jurisdiction (Arts 34–41)

**Art 34 — Basic jurisdictional structure**:
- HK courts (CFI, CA, CFA) hear NSL cases in the first instance

**Art 36 — Police powers**:
- The National Security Department of the HK Police (NSD) investigates NSL offences
- NSD may arrest, detain, search premises, intercept communications, and freeze assets

**Art 41 — HK court jurisdiction (standard)**:
- NSL offences are tried in the Court of First Instance
- Appeals follow the normal HK hierarchy (CA → CFA, with leave where required)
- The Secretary for Justice may issue a certificate confirming that acts do not involve national security (binding on the court)

**Art 55 — Central Government jurisdiction (exceptional)**:
The Central Government may exercise jurisdiction over an NSL case if:
1. (a) Foreign forces are complex and the HKSAR cannot effectively exercise jurisdiction
2. (b) A grave situation has arisen where the HKSAR cannot enforce the NSL
3. (c) A serious threat to national security has emerged requiring Central Government intervention

**Consequences of Art 55 invocation**:
- The case is handled by the Office for Safeguarding National Security (OSNS) of the Central Government
- The case proceeds in accordance with Mainland criminal procedure law
- The suspect/accused is transferred to Mainland authorities
- Legal representation rights under Mainland law apply (not HK law)

#### Trial Procedure (Arts 42–52)

**Art 42 — Bail**:
- **No presumption of bail** — an NSL suspect/accused may be released on bail only if there are "sufficient grounds" to believe they will not continue to endanger national security
- This is a significantly higher threshold than the ordinary HK bail test under s.9D of the Criminal Procedure Ordinance (Cap 221)
- The prosecution must be heard before any bail decision
- The court must consider the danger to national security above all else
- The Secretary for Justice may apply for review of any bail decision

**Art 44 — Designated Judges**:
- The Chief Executive appoints a panel of designated judges from the existing judiciary to hear NSL cases
- Designated judges must take an oath to uphold the Basic Law and the NSL
- The CE may remove a designated judge (no judicial immunity from removal in this context)
- Designated judges sit in the CFI for NSL trials

**Art 46 — Judge-only trial**:
- NSL cases are tried without a jury
- A panel of one or three designated judges hears the case and delivers judgment with reasons
- The Secretary for Justice may issue a certificate directing judge-only trial (in any case where jury trial would otherwise apply)
- The panel must provide written, reasoned judgments

**Art 47 — Certificate procedure**:
- Where a question arises as to whether an act involves national security, the Secretary for Justice must issue a certificate
- The certificate is **conclusive and binding on the courts**
- This is triggered when the SJ certifies the matter; the court cannot independently determine the national security character of an act

**Art 48 — Protection of sensitive evidence**:
- The court may hear evidence in closed session where national security is prejudiced
- Public interest immunity (PII) applications in NSL contexts
- Restrictions on disclosure of sensitive material

**Art 49 — Sanctions and disqualification**:
- In addition to imprisonment, the court may order disqualification from holding office (LegCo, public office) for up to 5 years beyond sentence
- Forfeiture of property connected to NSL offences

**Arts 50–52 — Appeals and finality**:
- Appeals follow the normal HK court hierarchy (CA → CFA)
- Where Art 55 jurisdiction is invoked, no HK court has appellate jurisdiction
- The SJ may appeal against sentence as of right

### Chapter IV — The Committee for Safeguarding National Security (Arts 53–59)

**Art 53 — Establishment**: The Committee for Safeguarding National Security of the HKSAR
- Chaired by the Chief Executive
- Members: Secretary for Justice, Secretary for Security, Commissioner of Police, Director of NSD, and other officials
- The Central Government's Office for Safeguarding National Security (OSNS) maintains liaison

**Art 54 — Functions**: 
- Analysing and assessing national security risks
- Making policy recommendations
- Coordinating law enforcement and prosecutorial action
- Reporting to the Central Government

**Art 55–59**: Relationship with the Central Government's OSNS

### Chapter V — Powers of the National Security Department (Arts 60–63)

**Art 60 — Institutional status**: The NSD is a department of the HK Police Force

**Art 61 — Powers of the NSD**: 
- Arrest, detention, search, and seizure
- Interception of communications and surveillance (subject to internal authorisation)
- Freezing of property and assets
- Investigation powers consistent with NSL objectives

**Art 62 — Cooperation obligations**: All HK government departments, public bodies, and statutory organisations must cooperate with the NSD

**Art 43 — Additional police powers (note: Art 43 is in Chapter III but cross-referenced)**:
- A police officer of the NSD may apply for a warrant from a designated magistrate for:
  - Interception of communications and surveillance (up to 48 hours without warrant in exigent circumstances)
  - Search of premises
  - Requiring production of materials
  - Detention for up to 48 hours (with extensions possible)
- These powers apply to persons reasonably suspected of NSL offences

### Chapter VI — Supplementary Provisions (Arts 64–66)

- **Art 64**: Relationship with Hong Kong domestic law — where NSL is inconsistent, NSL prevails
- **Art 65**: Extraterritorial application — NSL applies to acts committed outside Hong Kong by HK permanent residents or organisations incorporated in Hong Kong
- **Art 66**: Commencement — 30 June 2020

## Key Procedural Differences from Ordinary HK Criminal Law

| Aspect | Ordinary HK Law | NSL Regime |
|--------|----------------|------------|
| Jury trial | Yes (for indictable offences) | No (judge-only, Art 46) |
| Bail | Presumption of bail (s.9D Cap 221) | No presumption; "sufficient grounds" (Art 42) |
| Judicial appointment | Normal judicial appointments | Designated judges (Art 44, appointed/removable by CE) |
| SJ certificate | No equivalent | Art 47 certificate conclusive on courts |
| National security evidence | PII applications | Closed hearings, special disclosure rules |
| Who prosecutes | DOJ prosecutors; SJ consent for certain offences | NSD or DOJ; SJ may personally direct |
| Jurisdictional override | None | Art 55 — Central Government may assume jurisdiction |
| Disqualification | Limited to certain offences | Automatic disqualification from office (up to 5 extra years) |

## CFA NSL Jurisprudence

### HKSAR v Lai Chee Ying (2021) 24 HKCFAR 328
**Key holdings**:
- The NSL's bail regime (Art 42) is constitutional — the "no presumption of bail" standard is consistent with the Basic Law
- The court must apply the strict Art 42 test, not the ordinary Cap 221 standard
- The designated judge system does not violate judicial independence
- Judge-only trials under Art 46 are lawful

### Tam Sze Leung v Secretary for Justice (2022) 25 HKCFAR 334
**Key holdings**:
- Pre-trial restraint orders under the NSL are subject to judicial review
- The court must balance the protection of national security against the defendant's rights
- Procedural fairness requires the defendant to have sufficient information to challenge a restraint
- The SJ's certificate under Art 47 is conclusive on the national security question

### HKSAR v Ma Chun Wai (2023) 26 HKCFAR 1
**Key holdings**:
- Sentencing guidelines for NSL offences — seriousness measured by risk to national security
- Life imprisonment reserved for the most serious cases (principled secession/subversion leaders)
- Mitigating factors include cooperation and early guilty plea
- Aggravating factors include leadership role, planning, cross-border elements

### Re: Overseas Legal Practitioners (2023)
**Key holdings**:
- The CFA determined that the NSL does not contain a blanket prohibition on overseas legal practitioners appearing in NSL cases
- However, the court retains discretion and must consider national security implications
- The Art 47 certificate is relevant where the SJ certifies national security implications of foreign legal representation
- This remains an active area of development post-2023

## Relationship with Basic Law and Overlapping HK Offences

### NSL as Annex III National Law
- The NSL operates as an Annex III national law under Basic Law Art 18
- It has the status of an HK ordinance (Cap 599J) but takes precedence over any inconsistent HK law
- The NSL does not repeal overlapping HK offences — they coexist

### Overlapping HK Offences
Some NSL offences overlap with existing HK criminal offences:

| NSL Offence | Overlapping HK Offence(s) |
|-------------|--------------------------|
| Secession (Art 20) | Treason (Cap 200), Sedition (Cap 200) |
| Subversion (Art 22) | Conspiracy to cause explosion etc. |
| Terrorism (Art 24) | Offences Against the Person Ordinance (Cap 212), Dangerous Goods Ordinance (Cap 295) |
| Collusion (Art 29) | Treason (Cap 200 — applicable; may be pre-empted by NSL) |

**Practice implication**: The Secretary for Justice may choose to charge under either the NSL or the overlapping HK ordinance. The choice may affect procedural protections (jury, bail, sentencing) and the applicable procedural regime.

### Art 47 Certificate — Key Practical Consideration
When a question arises about whether an act involves national security, the SJ issues a certificate that is binding on the court. This means:
- The court cannot independently determine the national security character of an act
- The certificate is conclusive on the national security question
- The certificate only answers the national security question, not the guilt/innocence of the accused
- This procedure has been the subject of CFA jurisprudence (Tam Sze Leung)

## Professional Obligations

### Conflict with Cap 159 (Legal Practitioners Ordinance)
- The NSL imposes a duty not to use legal representation to endanger national security
- The Law Society of Hong Kong has issued practice directions on NSL compliance
- The Bar Association (HKBA) has issued statements on professional conduct in NSL cases
- Legal practitioners must balance duty of confidentiality (Cap 159) with obligations under the NSL
- Where a conflict arises, seek independent legal advice and consider senior counsel opinion

### Law Society Guidance (Key Points)
- Practitioners must take reasonable steps to understand NSL obligations
- Client advice should flag NSL risks where engaged
- Documentation of advice is critical
- Privilege remains fundamental but is limited where national security is engaged
- Reporting obligations may arise in certain circumstances

### HKBA Guidance (Key Points)
- Members must not engage in conduct that endangers national security
- Duty to the court remains paramount
- Professional independence must be maintained
- Fear of reprisal is not a valid basis for declining a brief (within bounds of competence)
- Seek advice from the Bar Council where uncertainty arises

## Practice Guidance Summary

When advising on NSL matters:

1. **Identify whether NSL is engaged** — examine the factual matrix for any national security nexus
2. **Assess jurisdiction** — is this an Art 41 (HK courts) or potential Art 55 (Central Government) case?
3. **Procedural implications** — bail (high threshold), no jury, designated judges, closed hearings
4. **Sentencing exposure** — life imprisonment potential; consider disqualification and forfeiture
5. **Evidence considerations** — PII, closed hearings, Art 47 certificate, sensitive evidence
6. **Overlapping HK offences** — consider charging alternatives and their procedural consequences
7. **Cross-border elements** — extraterritorial application (Art 65), foreign connections, Art 55 risk
8. **Professional obligations** — Law Society and HKBA guidance on NSL practice
